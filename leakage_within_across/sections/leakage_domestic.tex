We test two domestic reallocation margins. Within NACE 4-digit (\S\ref{sec:domestic_within_intensive}), we ask whether buyers shift purchases from more-exposed to less-exposed regulated suppliers in the same product market. Across NACE 4-digit (\S\ref{sec:domestic_across}), we ask whether buyers shift expenditure from regulated input categories to unregulated ones. The two margins are complementary: the within-NACE-4d margin captures substitution among firms that compete on essentially the same product, while the across-NACE-4d margin captures substitution among input bundles. A buyer that cannot find a cleaner steel supplier may still be able to redesign its product to use less steel and more aluminum.

For each margin we estimate both the intensive margin (the buyer's share of spending) and the extensive margin (whether the buyer purchases from the regulated source at all). The reallocation that the leakage hypothesis would predict --- a smaller share of regulated input or a higher probability of exiting the relationship --- does not appear on either margin or on either side.

\subsubsection{Within NACE 4-digit: intensive margin (across suppliers)}\label{sec:domestic_within_intensive}

We consider two natural treatment periods $\tau \in \{2005, 2017\}$: the launch of the EU ETS in 2005, and the 2015 Market Stability Reserve reform whose effective post-period begins in 2017. For each $\tau$ we build (buyer $b$, NACE 4-digit $n$) cells using pre-event data --- 2015--16 for the MSR specification, and 2005 itself for the EU ETS-launch specification (since the panel begins in 2005, we treat the policy's first year as a valid proxy for the pre-policy state under the assumption that one year is too short for firms to fully adjust). The post-period starts at 2017 and 2006 respectively. A cell is included if the buyer sourced from at least two distinct suppliers within that NACE 4-digit over the pre-period and at least one of them had a positive carbon-cost exposure $\omega$.\footnote{Firm-level exposure $\omega_j$ is the carbon cost the EU ETS imposes on firm $j$ as a share of its total cost: $\omega_j = \max(\text{emissions}_j - \text{free allocation}_j, 0) \cdot \text{EUA}_t / \text{total cost}_j$, averaged over the pre-event window. See Section~\ref{sec:data}.} Within each cell we identify the \emph{top-omega} supplier (the supplier with the largest pre-event $\omega$) and the \emph{bottom-omega} supplier (the smallest), and we track the share each captures of the buyer's spending in that NACE 4-digit over 2005--2022. The bottom-omega supplier is the within-cell control: in most cells it has $\omega = 0$ (a non-ETS firm, or an ETS firm with surplus allocation in the pre-period).

\paragraph{Specification.} The regression compares the within-cell evolution of the top-omega supplier's share to the bottom-omega supplier's share around the event year:
\begin{equation}
    \text{share}_{b,n,r,t} \;=\; \alpha_{b,n,r} \;+\; \delta_t \;+\; \beta \cdot \mathbf{1}[r = \text{top}] \cdot \mathbf{1}[t \geq \tau] \;+\; \varepsilon_{b,n,r,t},
    \label{eq:within_intensive}
\end{equation}
where $r \in \{\text{top}, \text{bot}\}$ indexes the supplier role within the cell, $\alpha_{b,n,r}$ is a cell-by-role fixed effect, and $\delta_t$ is a year fixed effect. Standard errors are clustered at the cell level. The coefficient $\beta$ measures the change in the top-omega supplier's within-cell share, post-event, relative to the bottom-omega supplier in the same cell. A leakage-consistent response is $\beta < 0$.

\paragraph{The most direct evidence of the null.} Figure~\ref{fig:within_intensive_topbot} reports the mean within-cell expenditure share of the top-omega and the bottom-omega supplier, across the two treatment periods. If buyers in our sample reallocated away from the top-omega supplier in response to the EU ETS, the two trajectories would separate post-event in the direction of the bottom-omega supplier. They do not. The two shares track each other through the post-event period in both windows, including across the post-MSR price run-up that pushed EUA prices from around \EUR{7}/tCO\textsubscript{2} to peaks above \EUR{80}/tCO\textsubscript{2}.

\paragraph{Where leakage should appear if it does.} The pooled trajectories may hide substitution that is concentrated in a subset of cells. Three natural cuts focus the test on the cells where leakage should be most visible. The first --- \emph{cost shock} --- restricts to cells where the carbon cost shock from the top-omega supplier represents the largest fraction of the buyer's total input bill, that is, the cells where the substitution incentive in euros is largest. The second --- \emph{input share} --- restricts to cells where the buyer spends the most on this narrowly defined input as a share of total cost, regardless of supplier carbon exposure: the cells where the input matters most to the buyer's cost structure. The third --- \emph{exposure gap} --- restricts to cells with the largest within-cell wedge in carbon exposure between the top-$\omega$ and bottom-$\omega$ supplier, that is, the cells where the policy creates the largest relative-price gap between the two within-cell competitors. For each of the three cuts we report two restrictions: the \emph{top quartile} ($\geq 75$th percentile of the cut variable, where leakage should be visible if it is operating on average) and the \emph{top decile} ($\geq 90$th percentile, where leakage should be visible if it is concentrated in the extreme tail). Table~\ref{tab:within_intensive_did} reports the DiD coefficient on (post $\times$ top-omega) from equation~(\ref{eq:within_intensive}) at the two treatment periods, pooled and under each of the six cut $\times$ restriction combinations.

\paragraph{Why is the substitution incentive so small?} The leakage hypothesis assumes that the policy creates a within-cell relative-price wedge that buyers can profit from by switching. Three quantities determine the size of that wedge --- the cost shock at the buyer's total cost level, the NACE 4-digit input share, and the within-cell $\omega$ gap --- and Figures~\ref{fig:within_dist_shock} and~\ref{fig:within_distributions_other} report their distributions across the cells in the 2017 treatment period (the larger of the two cell sets). The cost shock at the buyer's total cost (Figure~\ref{fig:within_dist_shock}) is the product of the top-$\omega$ supplier's carbon-cost intensity, the share of the buyer's total cost that goes to that supplier, and the end-of-2018 EUA price deflated to 2016 euros using the Belgian aggregate producer price index (\EUR{23.70}/tCO\textsubscript{2}). End-of-2018 is the first sustained post-MSR price: the market priced in the MSR over the course of 2018, before the formal activation in 2019. Appendix~\ref{app:within_dist_shock_robustness} reports the analogous distributions at the lower end-of-2017 price (\EUR{7.94}/tCO\textsubscript{2}, pre-MSR-peak) and the higher end-of-2022 price (\EUR{61.83}/tCO\textsubscript{2}, post-MSR-peak), and a version that scales the shock by the buyer's spending in the relevant NACE 4-digit sector instead of the buyer's total input cost. \notrun{The median cell faces a cost shock below X of its total input cost and the 99th percentile cell faces a shock below X.} The NACE 4-digit input share at the buyer (Figure~\ref{fig:within_dist_nshare}) is similarly right-skewed. The $\omega$ gap between top and bottom supplier (Figure~\ref{fig:within_dist_gap}) is, in most cells, an order of magnitude smaller than the supplier-level $\omega$ itself, because the bottom-$\omega$ supplier within a cell is usually a non-ETS firm with $\omega = 0$ whose own cost structure is nonetheless partly carbon-exposed through its own inputs.

\subsubsection{Across NACE 4-digit: input-category share}\label{sec:domestic_across}

The second domestic margin is the buyer's choice across input categories. When carbon pricing raises the average price of inputs in a regulated category, buyers may shift expenditure to less-regulated categories --- the steel-to-aluminum margin rather than the steel-supplier-A-to-steel-supplier-B margin. The intensive-margin outcome is the buyer's expenditure share in NACE 4-digit category $n$; the extensive-margin outcome is the indicator that the buyer sources from category $n$ at all.

\begin{equation}
    \text{share}_{b,n,t} \;=\; \beta\,\text{exposure}_n \times \mathbf{1}[t \geq 2015] \;+\; \alpha_{b,n} \;+\; \delta_{b,t} \;+\; \varepsilon_{b,n,t},
    \label{eq:domestic_across_intensive}
\end{equation}
with buyer-category fixed effects $\alpha_{b,n}$ and buyer-year fixed effects $\delta_{b,t}$. $\text{exposure}_n$ is the average pre-shock exposure of the ETS suppliers active in category $n$. Standard errors are two-way clustered on buyer and on NACE 4-digit. The buyer-year fixed effect $\delta_{b,t}$ absorbs any buyer-specific scale change, so $\beta$ identifies the post-2015 relative shift across categories within the same buyer-year. The extensive-margin analog replaces the share with an indicator and keeps the same fixed-effect block.

\paragraph{Headline result --- intensive margin.} \notrun{} $\widehat{\beta} = X$ (s.e.\ $X$, $p = X$). The post-2015 shift in the within-buyer expenditure share toward regulated categories is statistically indistinguishable from zero (Figure~\ref{fig:across_nace4d_intensive}). Buyers do not redesign their input mix away from regulated inputs as the EU ETS tightens.

Even at the highest-shortage end of the regulated-category distribution, the buyer's expenditure share does not respond.

\paragraph{Reading the two domestic margins together.} The within-NACE-4d and across-NACE-4d tests probe two distinct adjustment margins. The within margin captures supplier swaps; the across margin captures input-bundle redesign. Buyers do not exercise either. The joint pattern is what one would predict if both the relative-price signal seen by the buyer and the elasticity with which they respond to it are small --- but the pattern alone does not separate those two channels. We return to that in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}.
