We test two domestic reallocation margins. Within NACE 4-digit (\S\ref{sec:domestic_within}), we ask whether buyers shift purchases from more-exposed to less-exposed regulated suppliers in the same product market. Across NACE 4-digit (\S\ref{sec:domestic_across}), we ask whether buyers shift expenditure from regulated input categories to unregulated ones. The two margins are complementary: the within-NACE-4d margin captures substitution among firms that compete on essentially the same product, while the across-NACE-4d margin captures substitution among input bundles. A buyer that cannot find a cleaner steel supplier may still be able to redesign its product to use less steel and more aluminum.

For each margin we estimate both the intensive margin (the buyer's share of spending) and the extensive margin (whether the buyer purchases from the regulated source at all). The reallocation that the leakage hypothesis would predict --- a smaller share of regulated input or a higher probability of exiting the relationship --- does not appear on either margin or on either side.

\subsubsection{Within NACE 4-digit: across suppliers}\label{sec:domestic_within}

For each (buyer $b$, supplier-NACE-4d $n$) cell with at least one ETS supplier and at least two suppliers active over the pre-shock window, we identify $j^*(b,n)$ as the most-exposed ETS supplier in the cell --- the supplier with the largest predetermined exposure to the EU ETS shock, frozen at pre-2015 averages.\footnote{Predetermined exposure is constructed as $\omega_j \times (1 - a_j)$, where $\omega_j$ is firm $j$'s emission intensity (tCO\textsubscript{2} per euro of input cost) measured on 2013--2014 data, and $a_j$ is firm $j$'s pre-shock free-allocation share. See Section~\ref{sec:data}.} The intensive-margin outcome is $j^*$'s share of cell-level annual spending; the extensive-margin outcome is the indicator that $j^*$ remains active as a supplier to $b$ in year $t$.

The regression for the intensive margin is
\begin{equation}
    \text{share top}_{b,n,t} \;=\; \beta\,\omega_{j^*(b,n)} \times \mathbf{1}[t \geq 2015] \;+\; \alpha_{b,n} \;+\; \delta_{n,t} \;+\; \varepsilon_{b,n,t},
    \label{eq:domestic_within_intensive}
\end{equation}
with buyer-cell fixed effects $\alpha_{b,n}$ and supplier-NACE-4d $\times$ year fixed effects $\delta_{n,t}$. Standard errors are two-way clustered on buyer and on supplier-NACE 4-digit. The coefficient $\beta$ is the post-2015 change in the most-exposed supplier's share per unit of exposure intensity. A leakage-consistent response is $\beta < 0$: buyers cut the share they spend on the most-exposed regulated supplier as the policy tightens. The extensive-margin regression replaces the left-hand side with the indicator $\mathbf{1}[\text{spending}_{b \to j^*, t} > 0]$ and keeps the same fixed-effect structure.

\paragraph{Headline result --- intensive margin.} \notrun{} $\widehat{\beta} = X$ (s.e.\ $X$, $p = X$). The point estimate is statistically indistinguishable from zero. The corresponding event study (Figure~\ref{fig:within_nace4d_intensive_pooled}) shows pre-period coefficients that are flat through 2010--2014 and post-period coefficients that hover near zero through the 2022 EUA peak. The full price run-up from \EUR{7} to \EUR{90} does not move the share that exposed Belgian buyers spend on their most-exposed regulated supplier.

\paragraph{Heterogeneity --- where the shock bites hardest.} A natural concern with a pooled null is that the average effect masks substitution concentrated in the cells where the shock is large at the buyer's total-cost level. Section~\ref{sec:shock_magnitude} documented that shock intensity is highly skewed across cells. The right test of the magnitude-too-small hypothesis is to look inside the top quartile of buyer-level exposure: if the policy reallocates anywhere, it should be there. \notrun{} On the top exposure quartile, $\widehat{\beta}_{Q4} = X$ (s.e.\ $X$). The point estimate is no larger in magnitude than in the pooled sample, and remains statistically indistinguishable from zero (Figure~\ref{fig:within_nace4d_topQ}). The heaviest-shock cells do not reallocate more per unit of exposure than the lightly-affected ones.

\paragraph{Heterogeneity --- by event window.} The post-2015 EU ETS run-up was not monotonic; the policy regime tightened in three discrete steps (Phase II in 2008, Phase III in 2013, and the MSR-driven reset in 2015 that compounded into the 2017--2022 peak). We estimate the same specification around each cutoff (Figure~\ref{fig:within_nace4d_by_cutoff}) and find no reallocation around any of them. The 2008 and 2013 event studies are flat both before and after the cutoff; the 2017 cutoff isolates the post-MSR period at peak EUA prices and is also flat. The reallocation that should be most detectable around the largest jump in the EUA price is not there.

\paragraph{Placebo on unregulated cells.} If the absence of a reallocation response reflected a systematic data artefact rather than firm behavior, we would expect to see the same pattern in cells where no ETS supplier is present. The placebo specification, defined on cells with no ETS supplier in the same NACE 4-digit, is by construction flat (Figure~\ref{fig:within_nace4d_placebo}). The headline null is therefore not a mechanical feature of the design.

\subsubsection{Across NACE 4-digit: input-category share}\label{sec:domestic_across}

The second domestic margin is the buyer's choice across input categories. When carbon pricing raises the average price of inputs in a regulated category, buyers may shift expenditure to less-regulated categories --- the steel-to-aluminum margin rather than the steel-supplier-A-to-steel-supplier-B margin. The intensive-margin outcome is the buyer's expenditure share in NACE 4-digit category $n$; the extensive-margin outcome is the indicator that the buyer sources from category $n$ at all.

\begin{equation}
    \text{share}_{b,n,t} \;=\; \beta\,\text{exposure}_n \times \mathbf{1}[t \geq 2015] \;+\; \alpha_{b,n} \;+\; \delta_{b,t} \;+\; \varepsilon_{b,n,t},
    \label{eq:domestic_across_intensive}
\end{equation}
with buyer-category fixed effects $\alpha_{b,n}$ and buyer-year fixed effects $\delta_{b,t}$. $\text{exposure}_n$ is the average pre-shock exposure of the ETS suppliers active in category $n$. Standard errors are two-way clustered on buyer and on NACE 4-digit. The buyer-year fixed effect $\delta_{b,t}$ absorbs any buyer-specific scale change, so $\beta$ identifies the post-2015 relative shift across categories within the same buyer-year. The extensive-margin analog replaces the share with an indicator and keeps the same fixed-effect block.

\paragraph{Headline result --- intensive margin.} \notrun{} $\widehat{\beta} = X$ (s.e.\ $X$, $p = X$). The post-2015 shift in the within-buyer expenditure share toward regulated categories is statistically indistinguishable from zero (Figure~\ref{fig:across_nace4d_intensive}). Buyers do not redesign their input mix away from regulated inputs as the EU ETS tightens.

\paragraph{Heterogeneity --- by shortage.} A second sharpening uses supplier-side shortage --- the gap between the regulated firm's emissions and its free allocation --- as a sectoral proxy for how hard the EU ETS bites in each NACE 4-digit. High-shortage categories are categories where regulated suppliers face the largest dollar shock and would, under a leakage mechanism, be the ones losing buyer expenditure. \notrun{} $\widehat{\beta}^{Q4,\text{shortage}} = X$ on the intensive margin and $X$ on the extensive margin (Figure~\ref{fig:across_nace4d_by_shortage}). Even at the highest-shortage end of the regulated-category distribution, the buyer's expenditure share does not respond.

\paragraph{Reading the two domestic margins together.} The within-NACE-4d and across-NACE-4d tests probe two distinct adjustment margins. The within margin captures supplier swaps; the across margin captures input-bundle redesign. Buyers do not exercise either. The joint pattern is what one would predict if both the relative-price signal seen by the buyer and the elasticity with which they respond to it are small --- but the pattern alone does not separate those two channels. We return to that in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}.
