We test two domestic reallocation margins. Within NACE 4-digit (\S\ref{sec:domestic_within}), we ask whether buyers shift purchases from more-exposed to less-exposed regulated suppliers in the same product market. Across NACE 4-digit (\S\ref{sec:domestic_across}), we ask whether buyers shift expenditure from regulated input categories to unregulated ones. The two margins are complementary: the within-NACE-4d margin captures substitution among firms that compete on essentially the same product, while the across-NACE-4d margin captures substitution among input bundles. A buyer that cannot find a cleaner steel supplier may still be able to redesign its product to use less steel and more aluminum.

For each margin we estimate both the intensive margin (the buyer's share of spending) and the extensive margin (whether the buyer purchases from the regulated source at all). The reallocation that the leakage hypothesis would predict --- a smaller share of regulated input or a higher probability of exiting the relationship --- does not appear on either margin or on either side.

\subsubsection{Within NACE 4-digit: intensive margin (across suppliers)}\label{sec:domestic_within_intensive}

For each ETS event year $\tau \in \{2008, 2013, 2017\}$, we build (buyer $b$, NACE 4-digit $n$) cells using the two pre-event years (2006--07, 2011--12, 2015--16). A cell is included if the buyer sourced from at least two distinct suppliers within that NACE 4-digit over the pre-period and at least one of them had a positive carbon-cost exposure $\omega$.\footnote{Firm-level exposure $\omega_j$ is the carbon cost the EU ETS imposes on firm $j$ as a share of its total cost: $\omega_j = \max(\text{emissions}_j - \text{free allocation}_j, 0) \cdot \text{EUA}_t / \text{total cost}_j$, averaged over the two pre-event years. See Section~\ref{sec:data}.} Within each cell we identify the \emph{top-omega} supplier (the supplier with the largest pre-event $\omega$) and the \emph{bottom-omega} supplier (the smallest), and we track the share each supplier captures of the buyer's spending in that NACE 4-digit over 2005--2022. The bottom-omega supplier is the within-cell control: in most cells it has $\omega = 0$ (a non-ETS firm, or an ETS firm with surplus allocation in the pre-period).

\paragraph{Specification.} The regression compares the within-cell evolution of the top-omega supplier's share to the bottom-omega supplier's share around the event year:
\begin{equation}
    \text{share}_{b,n,r,t} \;=\; \alpha_{b,n,r} \;+\; \delta_t \;+\; \beta \cdot \mathbf{1}[r = \text{top}] \cdot \mathbf{1}[t \geq \tau] \;+\; \varepsilon_{b,n,r,t},
    \label{eq:within_intensive}
\end{equation}
where $r \in \{\text{top}, \text{bot}\}$ indexes the supplier role within the cell, $\alpha_{b,n,r}$ is a cell-by-role fixed effect, and $\delta_t$ is a year fixed effect. Standard errors are clustered at the cell level. The coefficient $\beta$ measures the change in the top-omega supplier's within-cell share, post-event, relative to the bottom-omega supplier in the same cell. A leakage-consistent response is $\beta < 0$.

\paragraph{Headline result.} Figure~\ref{fig:within_intensive_topbot} reports the mean share of the top-omega and bottom-omega supplier within their cells, across the three event windows. \notrun{} The two trajectories track each other closely throughout, including across the post-MSR price run-up. There is no visible separation post-event in the direction that the leakage hypothesis predicts.

\paragraph{DiD coefficients and heterogeneity cuts.} Table~\ref{tab:within_intensive_did} reports $\hat{\beta}$ from equation~(\ref{eq:within_intensive}) for each event year, both pooled across all cells and restricted to the top quartile under three heterogeneity cuts. The cuts isolate the cells where leakage should be most likely if it is operating at all. Top quartile by \emph{buyer-total shock} restricts to cells where the policy-induced cost shock from the top-omega supplier is the largest fraction of the buyer's total input bill --- the cells where the substitution incentive is largest. Top quartile by \emph{NACE 4-digit input share} restricts to cells where this NACE 4-digit input matters most to the buyer, regardless of its carbon exposure. Top quartile by \emph{$\omega$ gap} restricts to cells with the largest within-cell wedge in carbon exposure between top and bottom supplier --- the cells where the policy creates the largest relative-price gap to substitute against.

\paragraph{Reading the table.} At 2013 (Phase III auctioning), all four columns return negative point estimates, none statistically significant. At 2017 (post-MSR), all four columns are close to zero. At 2008 (Phase II onset), the four columns are positive --- the opposite of what leakage would predict --- which reflects mean reversion and a size-omega correlation that mixes with the omega channel in the early window where most ETS firms had surplus allocation.\footnote{Section 1 of [phase4 within-NACE-4d body of analyses] documents that the median Belgian ETS firm in 2011--12 had allocated allowances 1.33 times its emissions; only 14\% of firms had $\omega > 0$ in that pre-period.} The 2013 and 2017 windows are the substantive tests: in neither does the top-omega supplier lose share to its bottom-omega within-cell competitor, in pooled or in any of the three top-quartile cuts.

\paragraph{Why is the substitution incentive so small?} The leakage hypothesis assumes that the policy creates a within-cell relative-price wedge that buyers can profit from by switching. Figures~\ref{fig:within_dist_shock} through~\ref{fig:within_dist_gap} report the distribution of the three ingredients that determine the size of that wedge, across the cells in the 2017 sample.\footnote{The 2017 sample is the largest of the three event windows and reflects the post-MSR period, when EUA prices were highest. The 2013 and 2008 distributions track the same shape, on smaller cell counts.} The dollar shock at the buyer's total cost (Figure~\ref{fig:within_dist_shock}) --- the product of the top-omega supplier's carbon-cost intensity and the share of the buyer's total cost that goes to that supplier --- has a median below \notrun{X} and a 99th percentile below \notrun{X}. The NACE 4-digit input share at the buyer (Figure~\ref{fig:within_dist_nshare}) --- how much this input category matters at all --- is similarly skewed. The $\omega$ gap between top and bottom supplier (Figure~\ref{fig:within_dist_gap}) is, in most cells, an order of magnitude smaller than the supplier-level $\omega$ itself, because the bottom-omega supplier within a cell is usually a non-ETS firm with $\omega = 0$ but a supplier-level cost structure that is itself partly carbon-exposed through its own inputs.

\paragraph{How much could a buyer save by substituting?} Combining the three ingredients gives the potential savings, as a fraction of the buyer's total input cost, from substituting the entire top-omega spend to the bottom-omega supplier:
\begin{equation}
    \text{savings}_{b,n}(\rho,\, p^{\text{EUA}}) \;=\; \rho \cdot p^{\text{EUA}} \cdot \left(\omega_{\text{top}} - \omega_{\text{bot}}\right) \cdot \frac{\text{cell spend}_{b,n}}{\text{buyer total cost}_b},
    \label{eq:savings}
\end{equation}
where $\omega_j = \text{shortage}_j / \text{total cost}_j$ is the physical carbon intensity of firm $j$'s pre-event input bill (units: tCO\textsubscript{2} per euro), $p^{\text{EUA}}$ is the EUA price (euros per tCO\textsubscript{2}), and $\rho \in [0, 1]$ is the rate at which the supplier's carbon-cost shock is passed through into the buyer's price. Figure~\ref{fig:within_savings} reports the cumulative distribution of (\ref{eq:savings}) under two EUA-price scenarios: the 2017 annual mean (\EUR{5.76}/tCO\textsubscript{2}, pre-MSR price) and the 2022 annual mean (\EUR{80.24}/tCO\textsubscript{2}, post-MSR peak). Even at the 2022 EUA price and full pass-through, the median cell could save only \notrun{0.002}\% of its total input cost from a full substitution; the 99th-percentile cell could save \notrun{4.7}\%. At 2017 EUA prices, even the 99th-percentile cell could save only \notrun{0.34}\%. The substitution incentive is, for the bulk of cells, an order of magnitude smaller than any plausible cost of finding, vetting, and on-boarding an alternative supplier.

\subsubsection{Across NACE 4-digit: input-category share}\label{sec:domestic_across}

The second domestic margin is the buyer's choice across input categories. When carbon pricing raises the average price of inputs in a regulated category, buyers may shift expenditure to less-regulated categories --- the steel-to-aluminum margin rather than the steel-supplier-A-to-steel-supplier-B margin. The intensive-margin outcome is the buyer's expenditure share in NACE 4-digit category $n$; the extensive-margin outcome is the indicator that the buyer sources from category $n$ at all.

\begin{equation}
    \text{share}_{b,n,t} \;=\; \beta\,\text{exposure}_n \times \mathbf{1}[t \geq 2015] \;+\; \alpha_{b,n} \;+\; \delta_{b,t} \;+\; \varepsilon_{b,n,t},
    \label{eq:domestic_across_intensive}
\end{equation}
with buyer-category fixed effects $\alpha_{b,n}$ and buyer-year fixed effects $\delta_{b,t}$. $\text{exposure}_n$ is the average pre-shock exposure of the ETS suppliers active in category $n$. Standard errors are two-way clustered on buyer and on NACE 4-digit. The buyer-year fixed effect $\delta_{b,t}$ absorbs any buyer-specific scale change, so $\beta$ identifies the post-2015 relative shift across categories within the same buyer-year. The extensive-margin analog replaces the share with an indicator and keeps the same fixed-effect block.

\paragraph{Headline result --- intensive margin.} \notrun{} $\widehat{\beta} = X$ (s.e.\ $X$, $p = X$). The post-2015 shift in the within-buyer expenditure share toward regulated categories is statistically indistinguishable from zero (Figure~\ref{fig:across_nace4d_intensive}). Buyers do not redesign their input mix away from regulated inputs as the EU ETS tightens.

\paragraph{Reading the two domestic margins together.} The within-NACE-4d and across-NACE-4d tests probe two distinct adjustment margins. The within margin captures supplier swaps; the across margin captures input-bundle redesign. Buyers do not exercise either. The joint pattern is what one would predict if both the relative-price signal seen by the buyer and the elasticity with which they respond to it are small --- but the pattern alone does not separate those two channels. We return to that in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}.
