A precondition for either margin of leakage is that carbon costs reach buyers as a relative-price increase on regulated suppliers. We document this precondition on the Belgian panel using the \citet{kanzig2023unequal} structural carbon-policy shock in two complementary specifications: an aggregate local-projection of Belgian PPI on the shock (the direct PPI analog of \citet{kanzig2023unequal}'s reduced-form HICP regression), and a panel local-projection that interacts the shock with sector-level emission intensity (the cross-sectional gradient that maps the aggregate response into a per-emission-intensity pass-through). A regulated-vs-unregulated event-study replication of \citet{CosterMejeanDiGiovanni2024} on Belgian monthly PPI is reported as a robustness exhibit in Appendix~\ref{app:passthrough_cmdj}.

\subsection{Aggregate response: Belgian PPI on the carbon-policy shock}\label{sec:passthrough_aggregate}

We first run the direct PPI analog of \citet{kanzig2023unequal}'s HICP IRF (his Step~6 reduced-form local projection for outcomes outside the SVAR). Aggregate Belgian PPI is constructed as the equally-weighted geometric mean of NACE 4-digit PPI; we then estimate, separately at each horizon $h \in \{0,1,\ldots,36\}$,
\begin{equation}
    \log \text{PPI}^{\text{agg}}_{m+h} - \log \text{PPI}^{\text{agg}}_{m-1} \;=\; \alpha_h + \beta_h \cdot \text{CPShock}_m + \varepsilon_{m,h},
    \label{eq:passthrough_aggregate}
\end{equation}
with Newey--West standard errors of lag $h+1$. $\beta_h$ is the cumulative percentage response of aggregate Belgian PPI to a unit Käenzig structural shock $h$ months out; the unit shock is sign-normalised so that EU-area HICP energy rises by approximately 1\% on impact (Käenzig's normalisation convention).

\paragraph{Result.} Figure~\ref{fig:passthrough_aggregate} plots $\widehat{\beta}_h$ for the headline sample 2008m1--2019m12. Aggregate Belgian PPI rises by approximately 0.05\% on impact, peaks near 0.22\% at $h = 6$, and plateaus around 0.20\% from $h = 6$ through $h = 24$ before decaying. The 68\% confidence band excludes zero at fifteen horizons. The peak magnitude is the same order as \citet{kanzig2023unequal}'s HICP-headline peak ($\sim 0.2$--$0.3$\%) — Belgian PPI passes through the carbon-policy shock at roughly the same scale as euro-area headline inflation, with a similar hump-shaped profile. The post-$h=24$ negative tail is consistent with mean-reversion of the temporary policy news. Robustness on 2005m1--2019m12 and 2013m1--2019m12 separately is in Appendix~\ref{app:passthrough_aggregate_robust}; both subsamples produce point estimates of the same shape and magnitude with wider confidence bands.

\subsection{Sectoral gradient: panel LP with emission-intensity exposure}\label{sec:passthrough_sectoral}

Aggregate pass-through pins down the average response but is silent on cross-sectional heterogeneity — whether sectors with high direct ETS exposure pass through more than sectors with low exposure. To recover that gradient we estimate the panel local-projection following \citet[eq.~10]{kanzig2023unequal}:
\begin{equation}
    \log \text{PPI}_{s,m+h} - \log \text{PPI}_{s,m-1} \;=\; \gamma_h \cdot \big(\omega_{s, t(m)} \times \text{CPShock}_m\big) \;+\; \sum_{j=1}^{6} \theta_{h,j} \log \text{PPI}_{s, m-j} \;+\; \alpha_s \;+\; \delta_m \;+\; \varepsilon_{s,m,h}
    \label{eq:passthrough_panel}
\end{equation}
separately at each horizon $h \in \{0, 1, \ldots, 36\}$ months, with sector and year-month fixed effects and standard errors clustered on NACE 4-digit. The year-month fixed effects $\delta_m$ absorb any aggregate response to the shock (including the response identified by equation~\ref{eq:passthrough_aggregate}), so $\gamma_h$ identifies the \emph{differential} response of high-$\omega$ vs low-$\omega$ sectors.

The treatment intensity $\omega_{s, t(m)}$ is the sector's predetermined direct emission intensity:
\begin{equation}
    \omega_{s, t(m)} \;=\; \frac{\sum_{j \in \mathcal{E}_s} \text{emissions}_{j, t(m)-1}}{\sum_{j \in \mathcal{E}_s} \text{total cost}_{j, t(m)-1}},
    \label{eq:omega}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{E}_s$ is the set of ETS firms in sector $s$ and $t(m)$ is the calendar year of month $m$. $\omega$ is a physical intensity (tCO\textsubscript{2} per EUR of operating cost), measured at year $t-1$ so it is predetermined relative to month $m$, and updates annually as emissions and cost evolve. $\omega$ does not multiply emissions by the prevailing EUA price: the EUA-price channel enters through $\text{CPShock}_m$, while $\omega$ measures only the physical exposure that translates an EUA-price news into a sector cost-impact.\footnote{Under marginal-cost pricing this is the relevant cross-sectional gradient regardless of free allocation: the opportunity cost of emissions is gross emissions $\times$ EUA price, independent of how allowances are distributed (\citealp{fabra2014}). Removing the EUA factor from $\omega$ also avoids the period-2007 attenuation problem: in Phase~I when EUA collapsed to near zero, an EUA-weighted $\omega$ would collapse mechanically across all sectors, eliminating the cross-sectional variation needed to identify $\gamma_h$. The physical-intensity convention preserves cross-sectional dispersion across all years.} The headline sample is 2006m1--2019m12 in principle, restricted to 2008m1--2019m12 here because Phase~I (2006--2007) adds little identifying variation: with EUA prices near zero in 2007, sectoral PPI does not respond materially to CPShock in those months regardless of $\omega$, attenuating $\widehat{\gamma}_h$ toward zero. $\omega$ is set to zero for non-ETS NACE 4-digit sectors; they contribute to fixed-effect estimation but not to identification of $\gamma_h$.

\paragraph{Interpretation.} $\widehat{\gamma}_h$ is the coefficient on the regressor $(\omega_s \times \text{CPShock}_m)$, with units of log-PPI per (tCO\textsubscript{2}/EUR) per unit CPShock. The cumulative log-PPI response of sector $s$ at horizon $h$ to a unit shock is $\widehat{\gamma}_h \cdot \omega_s$. For a typical Phase~III ETS-active sector with $\omega \approx 1\times 10^{-3}$ tCO\textsubscript{2}/EUR, $\widehat{\gamma}_h = 1$ corresponds to a 0.1\% PPI rise per unit CPShock at horizon $h$; for a high-intensity sector (cement, steel) with $\omega \approx 5\times 10^{-3}$, the same $\widehat{\gamma}_h$ implies a 0.5\% rise.

\paragraph{Result.} Figure~\ref{fig:passthrough_gamma} reports $\widehat{\gamma}_h$ for 2008m1--2019m12 with 68\% and 90\% confidence bands. The point estimate is positive at 36 of 37 horizons, rises from $\widehat{\gamma}_0 \approx 0.05$ on impact, builds gradually, peaks near $\widehat{\gamma}_h = 1.15$ at $h = 22$, and plateaus in the range $\widehat{\gamma}_h \in [0.7, 1.1]$ from $h = 24$ through $h = 36$. Eleven horizons are significantly different from zero at 68\%, clustered at the post-$h=24$ plateau. The shape mirrors the aggregate IRF — gradual buildup, peak around two years, plateau at long horizons — confirming that the aggregate pass-through is concentrated in (rather than uniform across) the high-emission-intensity cross-section. Wide confidence bands at intermediate horizons reflect the same LP-IV power problem flagged by \citet[App.~C.5]{kanzig2023unequal} for monthly carbon-policy outcomes.

\paragraph{Sample-window robustness.} Appendix~\ref{app:passthrough_aggregate_robust} reports the aggregate IRF on 2005m1--2019m12 (full Käenzig window including Phase~I) and 2013m1--2019m12 (Phase~III only). Appendix~\ref{app:passthrough_gamma_phase3} reports the sectoral panel-LP $\widehat{\gamma}_h$ on 2013m1--2019m12. The 2013m1--2019m12 panel-LP is statistically indistinguishable from the headline 2008m1--2019m12 estimate at all but one horizon (mean $|z|$ across horizons = 0.54, only one horizon with $|z| > 1.96$), but the smaller sample (84 vs 144 months; 479 vs 821 sector-year cells with $\omega > 0$) inflates standard errors by a factor of $\sim 1.7$, widening confidence bands enough that the negative point estimates at long horizons are within sampling noise rather than evidence against pass-through.

\paragraph{Anchor for the rest of the paper.} The aggregate IRF and the sectoral $\gamma_h$ are mutually consistent and indicate positive pass-through of carbon-policy shocks to Belgian PPI at horizons of one to three years. Section~\ref{sec:domestic_within} uses $\rho = 1$ as the central case and $\rho = 0.5$ as a lower bound on the buyer-side relative-price signal in the CES mapping.
