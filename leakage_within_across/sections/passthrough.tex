%       Belgian manufacturing prices, persistently;

%       higher allowance shortage relative to total cost (DiD column C);

This section asks two simple questions about Belgian producer prices and the EU ETS. First, when EUA prices move because of an identifiable carbon-policy shock, do manufacturing prices move with them, and does the response persist? Second, are the price increases concentrated in the sectors that one would expect, ex ante, to be most exposed? We do not attempt to pin down the size of pass-through; we only ask whether the qualitative patterns are present.

% ----------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{The aggregate price response}\label{sec:passthrough_aggregate}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------

We use the monthly carbon-policy shock series of \citet{kanzig2023unequal} to study how aggregate Belgian manufacturing prices respond to identifiable innovations in EUA prices. The series is constructed to isolate the surprise component of EUA-price moves around regulatory announcements; it is the workhorse instrument in the recent literature on aggregate carbon-pricing pass-through.

We estimate, at monthly horizons $h = 0, \dots, 24$,
\begin{equation}
    \log P^{\text{agg}}_{m+h} - \log P^{\text{agg}}_{m-1} \;=\; \alpha_h + \gamma_h \cdot \text{CPShock}_m + \sum_{j=1}^{6} \phi_{h,j} \cdot \log P^{\text{agg}}_{m-j} + \varepsilon_{m, h},
    \label{eq:passthrough_aggregate}
\end{equation}
where $P^{\text{agg}}_m$ is the equally-weighted geometric mean of NACE 4-digit Belgian PPI in month $m$, on the sample 2005m1--2019m12. The six lags of aggregate log-PPI on the right-hand side soak up LHS persistence in the local projection without affecting identification of $\gamma_h$ \citep[][Plagborg-M\o ller--Wolf augmentation]{oleaplagborgmoller2019}. Standard errors are Newey--West with lag $h+1$.

The point estimate is positive at every horizon. It rises through the first six months, dips slightly through the end of the first year, and rises again into the second year. The 90\% confidence band excludes zero at horizons $h = 5, 6, 7$ and again at $h = 10$. Manufacturing prices go up after a carbon-policy shock, and they remain elevated through two years. We treat this as one qualitative fact: the aggregate response is positive and persistent.

% ----------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Heterogeneity in the response}\label{sec:passthrough_het}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------

We next ask whether sectors that were more exposed ex ante saw larger price increases. We use 2017 --- the year EUA prices began their sustained rise after the October 2015 Market Stability Reserve decision --- as the cutoff, and build pre-2017 sector-level exposure measures from EUTL records joined to firm accounts.

For each NACE 4-digit sector $s$ we construct, on 2015--2016, the ratios
\begin{align}
    \omega^{em}_s   &= \frac{\sum_{i \in s, \, t \in 2015\text{--}16} \text{emissions}_{i,t}}{\sum_{i \in s, \, t \in 2015\text{--}16} \text{total\_cost}_{i,t}}, \\
    \omega^{sh}_s   &= \frac{\sum_{i \in s, \, t \in 2015\text{--}16} \text{shortage}_{i,t}}{\sum_{i \in s, \, t \in 2015\text{--}16} \text{total\_cost}_{i,t}},
\end{align}
where $\text{shortage}_{i,t} = \max(\text{emissions}_{i,t} - \text{free\_allocation}_{i,t}, \, 0)$ and the inner sum runs over ETS firms $i$ in $s$. Both measures are physical intensities (tCO\textsubscript{2} per EUR of operating cost); the EUA price enters the regressions only through the post-2017 indicator interaction in \eqref{eq:did_2017}, not through $\omega$. We also use a binary regulated-vs-unregulated treatment indicator $\text{Treated}_s$ that flags 4-digit sectors whose 2-digit parent contains regulated installations.

For each exposure measure $X_s \in \{\text{Treated}_s, \omega^{em}_s, \omega^{sh}_s\}$ we estimate
\begin{equation}
    \log P_{s,m} \;=\; \beta \cdot X_s \cdot \mathbf{1}\{\text{year}(m) \geq 2017\} + \alpha_s + \delta_m + \varepsilon_{s,m}
    \label{eq:did_2017}
\end{equation}
on the monthly NACE 4-digit panel of Belgian PPI from 2010m1, with sector and year-month fixed effects and standard errors clustered at the NACE 4-digit level. Specifications (A), (B), and (C) substitute the binary, the emissions, and the shortage measures respectively. We report (C) on two windows: ending 2019m12 and ending 2022m12.

Table~\ref{tab:passthrough_did} reports the coefficient $\hat\beta$ in each column. Two observations carry the section.

First, in column (C), sectors with higher pre-2017 \emph{shortage} exposure show a higher price level from 2017 onward, on both windows. The differential is statistically distinguishable from zero on the 2010--2019 window --- before the 2021--22 EUA price spike, before the COVID disruption, and before the energy crisis. We read this as our second qualitative finding: \emph{when we sort sectors by how much of their cost base went into buying allowances on the market, the more-exposed sectors see larger price increases after 2017}.

Second, column (B) cannot reject the null. When we sort sectors instead by emissions-over-cost --- a measure that includes emissions covered by free allocation --- the post-2017 differential is indistinguishable from zero. Because shortage and emissions are correlated across sectors, but a sector can have large emissions and very little shortage when its free allocation is generous, the two measures are not interchangeable. The contrast between columns (B) and (C) is consistent with this: the empirically relevant margin of cross-sector variation is the shortage that the sector had to absorb on the market, not the gross emissions count.

Figure~\ref{fig:passthrough_eventstudy} traces the same exercise year by year. We replace the post-2017 indicator in \eqref{eq:did_2017} with a full set of year-specific interactions $\sum_{\tau \neq 2016} X_s \cdot \mathbf{1}\{\text{year}(m) = \tau\}$, dropping 2016 as the reference. The figure plots $\hat\beta_\tau$ for column (C) on the 2010m1--2022m12 sample. Pre-2017 coefficients are noisy and centered on zero; coefficients from 2017 onward are positive and rise through the sample. The pattern matches the table: the differential is a post-2017 phenomenon, not a pre-existing trend.

% ----------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Summary}\label{sec:passthrough_summary}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------

We document three qualitative facts. Aggregate Belgian manufacturing prices respond positively and persistently to identifiable carbon-policy shocks. Sectors that, prior to 2017, were buying a larger fraction of their cost base in allowances on the market saw larger price increases from 2017 onward. Sorting sectors by gross emissions exposure delivers a null. We make no claim about the magnitude of pass-through, and we do not interpret these patterns beyond what they say: when EUA prices rise, manufacturing prices rise too, and they rise more in the sectors with greater allowance-deficit exposure. Robustness exercises around alternative shock measures, alternative treatment dates, and post-treatment trend sensitivity are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:passthrough}.
