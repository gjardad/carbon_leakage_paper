A precondition for either margin of leakage is that carbon costs reach buyers as a relative-price increase on regulated suppliers. We document this precondition on the Belgian panel under three identification strategies and use the resulting magnitudes to anchor the CES mapping in \S\ref{sec:domestic_within} and to interpret the across-country results in \S\ref{sec:international}.

\paragraph{Strategy 1: monthly panel local projections on the Känzig structural carbon-policy shock.} On a 2005m1--2019m12 monthly NACE 4-digit PPI panel, we estimate the local-projection specification
\begin{equation}
    \log \text{PPI}_{s,m+h} - \log \text{PPI}_{s,m-1} \;=\; \gamma_h \cdot \big(\text{CPShock}_m \times \text{intensity}_s^{\text{base}}\big) \;+\; \alpha_s \;+\; \delta_m \;+\; \varepsilon_{s,m,h}
    \label{eq:passthrough_strategy1}
\end{equation}
separately at each horizon $h \in \{0, 1, \ldots, 36\}$ months. $\text{CPShock}_m$ is the \citet{kanzig2023unequal} carbon-policy structural shock (VAR-identified surprise, monthly), $\alpha_s$ and $\delta_m$ are sector and year-month fixed effects, and standard errors are clustered on NACE 4-digit.

The treatment intensity $\text{intensity}_s^{\text{base}}$ is the sector's pre-shock cost-share exposure to ETS costs, defined as
\begin{equation}
    \text{intensity}_s^{\text{base}} \;=\; \frac{1}{4}\sum_{t = 2013}^{2016} \frac{\sum_{j \in \mathcal{E}_s} \text{shortage}_{j,t} \cdot \text{EUA}_t}{\overline{\sum_{j \in \mathcal{E}_s} \text{total cost}_{j,t}}_{\,t \in 2010..2012}},
    \label{eq:intensity_base}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{E}_s$ is the set of ETS firms in sector $s$, the numerator is the sector's average annual realized carbon cost over 2013--2016 (Phase~III, post-auctioning), and the denominator is the sector's total operating cost averaged over 2010--2012 (a pre-shock baseline). The ratio answers: ``what fraction of sector $s$'s pre-shock operating cost was paid as ETS allowance cost on average over 2013--2016?'' It is dimensionless, predetermined relative to the 2015 MSR cutoff, and varies cross-sectionally from zero (sectors with no ETS bite) to roughly two percent in the most-exposed Belgian sectors (basic chemicals, cement, metals).

\paragraph{Interpretation of $\widehat{\gamma}_h$.} For sector $s$ with intensity $\text{intensity}_s^{\text{base}} = x$, and a unit positive CPShock at month $m$, equation~(\ref{eq:passthrough_strategy1}) implies the cumulative log-PPI response over the $h+1$ months from $m-1$ to $m+h$ is approximately $\widehat{\gamma}_h \cdot x$. To translate to a percentage-point PPI response, multiply $\widehat{\gamma}_h$ by the intensity in question. For example, at $\widehat{\gamma}_{12} \approx 1.22$ and a sector with $\text{intensity}_s^{\text{base}} = 0.01$ (a top-decile-exposed Belgian sector), a unit CPShock at month $m$ raises the sector's PPI by about $1.22 \times 0.01 = 1.2$\% at horizon $h = 12$ months. $\widehat{\gamma}_h$ should be read as the heterogeneous treatment-effect slope of pass-through with respect to sector cost-share exposure: it is large at sectors where the shock bites hard.

Figure~\ref{fig:ppi_lp_kanzig_style} reports $\widehat{\gamma}_h$. The IRF builds gradually from $\widehat{\gamma}_0 \approx 0.17$ on impact, crosses unity around $h = 12$ months ($\widehat{\gamma}_{12} = 1.22$, s.e.\ $0.22$), and peaks at $\widehat{\gamma}_{18} \approx 1.76$ before fading slowly through $h = 36$. The shape (modest on impact, peak around $h = 15$--$18$, slow fade) is the Belgian-PPI analog of the aggregate-HICP IRF in \citet{kanzig2023unequal}.

\paragraph{Strategy 2: regulated-vs-unregulated event study at NACE 4-digit, with sector-specific linear trends.} Following \citet{colmeretal2023}, we estimate
\begin{equation}
    \log \text{PPI}_{s,t} \;=\; \sum_\tau \beta_\tau\,\mathbf{1}[s \in \text{regulated}]\,\mathbf{1}[\text{year} = \tau] \;+\; \alpha_s \;+\; \delta_t \;+\; \theta_s \cdot t \;+\; \varepsilon_{s,t}
    \label{eq:passthrough_strategy2}
\end{equation}
on the 2001--2022 annual NACE 4-digit panel with sector-specific linear trends $\theta_s \cdot t$. Figure~\ref{fig:cmdj_ppi_eventstudy} reports the year-by-year $\widehat{\beta}_\tau$ on the Belgian panel, mimicking \citet{CosterMejeanDiGiovanni2024} Figure 1 on French INSEE PPI data. Phase-aggregated coefficients (CMdG-tight treatment of seven NACE 2-digit codes, the same set as in \citet{CosterMejeanDiGiovanni2024}'s Table A.5):
\[
    \widehat{\beta}_{\text{Phase 2}} \;=\; +0.094\,(***), \qquad \widehat{\beta}_{\text{Phase 4}} \;=\; +0.113\,(**).
\]
Phase 1 and Phase 3 coefficients collapse to insignificance once sector trends are absorbed; we report Phase 2 and Phase 4 because those are the periods where regulated PPI rises differentially after netting out pre-existing sector-level dynamics.

\paragraph{Strategy 3: descriptive OLS with sector-specific trends.} The annual exposure-share elasticity is small ($+0.07$ in S6 with sector trends) and not significantly different from zero on a single-shock specification --- consistent with the literature \citep[e.g.,][]{martin2014} that finds firm-level pass-through dispersed and noisy in the cross-section. The strongest piece of identifiable evidence remains Strategy 1.

\paragraph{Anchor for the rest of the paper.} For the elasticity bounds in \S\ref{sec:domestic_within} we use the CMdG-tight estimate as the average post-2015 regulated-vs-unregulated log-price differential, magnitude $\approx 0.10$ (\ie about 10 log-points cumulative over Phase~2 and Phase~4). At the right tail, sector-level pass-through is consistent with full pass-through under Strategy 1; we use $\rho = 1$ as an upper bound and $\rho = 0.5$ as a lower bound on the buyer-side relative-price signal.
