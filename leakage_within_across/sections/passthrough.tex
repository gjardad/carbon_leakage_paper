% magnitude for pass-through; we document only:
%   (1) shocks of the same kind as the carbon-policy shock raise overall
%       Belgian manufacturing prices, persistently;
%   (2) the price increase is larger for sectors that, before 2017, had
%       higher allowance shortage relative to total cost (DiD column C);
%   (3) we cannot reject the null of no differential price increase when
%       sector exposure is measured by emissions-over-cost rather than by
%       shortage-over-cost (DiD column B).

This section asks two simple questions about Belgian producer prices and the EU ETS. First, when EUA prices move because of an identifiable carbon-policy shock, do manufacturing prices move with them, and does the response persist? Second, are the price increases concentrated in the sectors that one would expect, ex ante, to be most exposed? We do not attempt to pin down the size of pass-through; we only ask whether the qualitative patterns are present.

% ----------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{The aggregate price response}\label{sec:passthrough_aggregate}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------

We use the monthly carbon-policy shock series of \citet{kanzig2023unequal} to study how aggregate Belgian manufacturing prices respond to identifiable innovations in EUA prices. The series is constructed to isolate the surprise component of EUA-price moves around regulatory announcements; it is the workhorse instrument in the recent literature on aggregate carbon-pricing pass-through.

We estimate, at monthly horizons $h = 0, \dots, 24$,
\begin{equation}
    \log P^{\text{agg}}_{m+h} - \log P^{\text{agg}}_{m-1} \;=\; \alpha_h + \gamma_h \cdot \text{CPShock}_m + \sum_{j=1}^{6} \phi_{h,j} \cdot \log P^{\text{agg}}_{m-j} + \varepsilon_{m, h},
    \label{eq:passthrough_aggregate}
\end{equation}
where $P^{\text{agg}}_m$ is the equally-weighted geometric mean of NACE 4-digit Belgian PPI in month $m$, on the sample 2005m1--2019m12. The six lags of aggregate log-PPI on the right-hand side soak up LHS persistence in the local projection without affecting identification of $\gamma_h$ \citep[][Plagborg-M\o ller--Wolf augmentation]{oleaplagborgmoller2019}. Standard errors are Newey--West with lag $h+1$.

The point estimate is positive at every horizon. It rises through the first six months, dips slightly through the end of the first year, and rises again into the second year. The 90\% confidence band excludes zero at horizons $h = 5, 6, 7$ and again at $h = 10$. Manufacturing prices go up after a carbon-policy shock, and they remain elevated through two years. We treat this as one qualitative fact: the aggregate response is positive and persistent.

% ----------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Heterogeneity in the response}\label{sec:passthrough_het}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------

We next ask whether sectors that were more exposed ex ante saw larger price increases. We use 2017 --- the year EUA prices began their sustained rise after the October 2015 Market Stability Reserve decision --- as the cutoff. For each NACE 4-digit sector $s$ we build a time-invariant pre-2017 exposure measure $X_s$ and estimate, on the monthly Belgian PPI panel from 2010m1,
\begin{equation}
    \log P_{s,m} \;=\; \beta \cdot X_s \cdot \mathbf{1}\{\text{year}(m) \geq 2017\} \;+\; \alpha_s \;+\; \delta_m \;+\; \varepsilon_{s,m},
    \label{eq:did_2017}
\end{equation}
with sector and year-month fixed effects and standard errors clustered at the NACE 4-digit level. We use three exposure measures: a binary indicator $\text{Treated}_s$ that flags 4-digit sectors whose 2-digit parent contains regulated installations; sector emissions per EUR of operating cost, $\omega^{em}_s$; and sector \emph{unallocated} emissions per EUR of cost, $\omega^{sh}_s$ (where the numerator nets out free allocation). Both $\omega$ measures are built from EUTL records joined to firm accounts on 2015--2016. Full definitions and sample windows are in the notes to Table~\ref{tab:passthrough_did}.

Because the three exposure measures live on completely different scales --- a binary indicator versus physical intensities in tCO\textsubscript{2}/EUR --- the raw $\hat\beta$ values are not comparable across columns. We therefore report $\hat\beta \times \text{SD}(X_s)$, where $\text{SD}(X_s)$ is the cross-sectional standard deviation of $X_s$ across the 134 NACE 4-digit sectors in the panel. The reported quantity is the \emph{implied post-2017 differential in log-PPI for a one-standard-deviation increase in cross-sectional exposure}. The statistical content is unchanged --- the $t$-statistic is invariant to the rescaling --- but magnitudes can now be read across columns on a common scale.

Two observations carry the section.

First, sorting sectors by allowance-shortage exposure (columns 3 and 4) delivers a positive and statistically distinguishable post-2017 differential on both sample windows. A sector one cross-sectional standard deviation higher in pre-2017 $\omega^{sh}$ has log-PPI roughly $0.012$ higher on the 2010m1--2019m12 window and $0.023$ higher on the 2010m1--2022m12 window --- between one and two percentage points higher prices per standard-deviation increase in shortage exposure. The differential is detectable on the pre-COVID, pre-2021-22 window and roughly doubles when we extend to 2022m12. We read this as our second qualitative finding: \emph{sectors that bought a larger fraction of their cost base in allowances on the market see larger price increases after 2017}.

Second, sorting sectors by gross emissions exposure (column 2) delivers a null. The point estimate is positive but indistinguishable from zero. Because $\omega^{em}_s$ and $\omega^{sh}_s$ are correlated across sectors but not identical --- a sector can have large emissions and very little shortage when its free allocation is generous --- the contrast between column 2 and columns 3--4 is informative: the empirically relevant margin of cross-sector variation is the shortage that the sector had to absorb on the market, not the gross emissions count.

Figure~\ref{fig:passthrough_eventstudy} traces the same exercise year by year. We replace the post-2017 indicator in \eqref{eq:did_2017} with a full set of year-specific interactions $\sum_{\tau \neq 2016} X_s \cdot \mathbf{1}\{\text{year}(m) = \tau\}$, dropping 2016 as the reference. The figure plots $\hat\beta_\tau$ for $X_s = \omega^{sh}_s$ on the 2010m1--2022m12 sample (column 4 of Table~\ref{tab:passthrough_did}). Pre-2017 coefficients are noisy and centered on zero; coefficients from 2017 onward are positive and rise through the sample. The pattern matches the table: the differential is a post-2017 phenomenon, not a pre-existing trend.

% ----------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Summary}\label{sec:passthrough_summary}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------

We document three qualitative facts. Aggregate Belgian manufacturing prices respond positively and persistently to identifiable carbon-policy shocks. Sectors that, prior to 2017, were buying a larger fraction of their cost base in allowances on the market saw larger price increases from 2017 onward. Sorting sectors by gross emissions exposure delivers a null. We make no claim about the magnitude of pass-through, and we do not interpret these patterns beyond what they say: when EUA prices rise, manufacturing prices rise too, and they rise more in the sectors with greater allowance-deficit exposure. Robustness exercises around alternative shock measures, alternative treatment dates, and post-treatment trend sensitivity are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:passthrough}.
