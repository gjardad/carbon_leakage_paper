A precondition for either margin of leakage is that carbon costs reach buyers as a relative-price increase on regulated suppliers. We document this precondition on the Belgian panel under three identification strategies and use the resulting magnitudes to anchor the CES mapping in \S\ref{sec:domestic_within} and to interpret the across-country results in \S\ref{sec:international}.

\paragraph{Strategy 1: monthly panel local projections on the Känzig structural carbon-policy shock.} Following the panel local-projection specification of \citet[eq.~10]{kanzig2023unequal}, on the 2010m1--2019m12 monthly NACE 4-digit PPI panel we estimate
\begin{equation}
    \log \text{PPI}_{s,m+h} - \log \text{PPI}_{s,m-1} \;=\; \gamma_h \cdot \big(\omega_{s, t(m)} \times \text{CPShock}_m\big) \;+\; \sum_{j=1}^{6} \theta_{h,j} \log \text{PPI}_{s, m-j} \;+\; \alpha_s \;+\; \delta_m \;+\; \varepsilon_{s,m,h}
    \label{eq:passthrough_strategy1}
\end{equation}
separately at each horizon $h \in \{0, 1, \ldots, 36\}$ months. $\text{CPShock}_m$ is the \citet{kanzig2023unequal} VAR-identified carbon-policy structural shock (refined event-day surprise, sign-normalised so a unit shock raises EUA prices on impact); $\omega_{s, t(m)}$ is sector $s$'s rolling cost-share exposure (defined below); $\sum_j \theta_{h,j} \log \text{PPI}_{s, m-j}$ is the lag-augmentation that controls for LHS persistence; $\alpha_s$ and $\delta_m$ are sector and year-month fixed effects; standard errors are clustered on NACE 4-digit. The sample window matches \citet{kanzig2023unequal}'s baseline VAR (1999m1--2019m12), trimmed at 2010m1 by ICAP daily EUA coverage.

The treatment intensity $\omega_{s, t(m)}$ is the sector's pre-shock marginal-cost-share exposure to ETS allowance costs, defined as a trailing two-year mean updating annually:
\begin{equation}
    \omega_{s, t(m)} \;=\; \frac{1}{2}\sum_{\tau \in \{t(m)-1, t(m)-2\}} \frac{\sum_{j \in \mathcal{E}_s} \text{emissions}_{j,\tau} \cdot \text{EUA}_\tau}{\sum_{j \in \mathcal{E}_s} \text{total cost}_{j,\tau}},
    \label{eq:intensity_base}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{E}_s$ is the set of ETS firms in sector $s$ and $t(m)$ is the calendar year of month $m$. The numerator uses gross emissions (not unallocated shortage) following the windfall-profit logic of \citet{fabra2014}: under marginal pricing, the relevant cost-share is the opportunity cost of emissions, which is gross emissions $\times$ EUA price regardless of free allocation. The construction is rolling rather than fixed because sector emission intensities evolve (technology improvements, scale changes), and the cross-sectional ranking of sectors by ETS exposure is not stable across the eight-year sample. $\omega_{s, t(m)}$ is set to zero for non-ETS NACE 4-digit sectors, which then contribute to the fixed-effect estimation but not to identification of $\gamma_h$.

\paragraph{Interpretation of $\widehat{\gamma}_h$.} Following \citet{kanzig2023unequal}'s normalisation convention, the panel-LP coefficient $\widehat{\gamma}_h$ is the cumulative log-PPI response over the $h+1$ months from $m-1$ to $m+h$ to a unit Käenzig structural carbon-policy shock at month $m$, scaled by sector cost-share exposure $\omega_{s, t(m)}$. A unit shock corresponds to a +1\% impact change in HICP energy prices in the underlying SVAR, which in turn corresponds to a sizable upward jump in EUA prices via Käenzig's first-stage identification. For sector $s$ with $\omega_s = 0.01$ (a top-decile-exposed Belgian manufacturing sector), a unit CPShock raises that sector's PPI cumulatively by approximately $\widehat{\gamma}_h \times 0.01$ over the next $h$ months relative to a sector with $\omega = 0$.

Figure~\ref{fig:ppi_lp_kanzig_style} reports $\widehat{\gamma}_h$. The IRF builds gradually from near zero on impact ($\widehat{\gamma}_0 = 0.005$, n.s.) to a first peak at $\widehat{\gamma}_9 = 0.10$ (s.e.\ $0.05$, $t = 2.10$) over the 9-month horizon, dips back near zero around $h = 14$--$20$, and rebuilds to a second peak at $\widehat{\gamma}_{33} = 0.13$ ($t = 2.42$) over the long horizon. For a top-decile sector ($\omega = 0.01$), peak-horizon pass-through implies a 0.1\% PPI rise per unit CPShock; for a sector at the most-exposed tail ($\omega \approx 0.05$, basic chemicals or cement at peak EUA-price years), the implied response is 0.5\%. The non-monotone shape reflects the limited statistical power of the 2010--2019 sample with only 39 sectors carrying non-trivial $\omega$; the qualitative pattern (positive, building over horizons of order one year) is consistent with the aggregate-HICP IRF in \citet{kanzig2023unequal}.

\paragraph{Strategy 2: regulated-vs-unregulated event study at NACE 4-digit, with sector-specific linear trends.} Following \citet{colmeretal2023}, we estimate
\begin{equation}
    \log \text{PPI}_{s,t} \;=\; \sum_\tau \beta_\tau\,\mathbf{1}[s \in \text{regulated}]\,\mathbf{1}[\text{year} = \tau] \;+\; \alpha_s \;+\; \delta_t \;+\; \theta_s \cdot t \;+\; \varepsilon_{s,t}
    \label{eq:passthrough_strategy2}
\end{equation}
on the 2001--2022 annual NACE 4-digit panel with sector-specific linear trends $\theta_s \cdot t$. Figure~\ref{fig:cmdj_ppi_eventstudy} reports the year-by-year $\widehat{\beta}_\tau$ on the Belgian panel, mimicking \citet{CosterMejeanDiGiovanni2024} Figure 1 on French INSEE PPI data. Phase-aggregated coefficients (CMdG-tight treatment of seven NACE 2-digit codes, the same set as in \citet{CosterMejeanDiGiovanni2024}'s Table A.5):
\[
    \widehat{\beta}_{\text{Phase 2}} \;=\; +0.094\,(***), \qquad \widehat{\beta}_{\text{Phase 4}} \;=\; +0.113\,(**).
\]
Phase 1 and Phase 3 coefficients collapse to insignificance once sector trends are absorbed; we report Phase 2 and Phase 4 because those are the periods where regulated PPI rises differentially after netting out pre-existing sector-level dynamics.

\paragraph{Strategy 3: descriptive OLS with sector-specific trends.} The annual exposure-share elasticity is small ($+0.07$ in S6 with sector trends) and not significantly different from zero on a single-shock specification --- consistent with the literature \citep[e.g.,][]{martin2014} that finds firm-level pass-through dispersed and noisy in the cross-section. The strongest piece of identifiable evidence remains Strategy 1.

\paragraph{Anchor for the rest of the paper.} For the elasticity bounds in \S\ref{sec:domestic_within} we use the CMdG-tight estimate as the average post-2015 regulated-vs-unregulated log-price differential, magnitude $\approx 0.10$ (\ie about 10 log-points cumulative over Phase~2 and Phase~4). At the right tail, sector-level pass-through is consistent with full pass-through under Strategy 1; we use $\rho = 1$ as an upper bound and $\rho = 0.5$ as a lower bound on the buyer-side relative-price signal.
