\subsection{The EU ETS in Belgium}

The EU Emissions Trading System has covered electricity generation and energy-intensive manufacturing installations across the EEA since 2005. Coverage is at the level of the regulated installation; we link installations to their parent firm via Belgian VAT identifiers. Phase~III (2013--2020) and Phase~IV (2021--) span our sample window. The Belgian ETS roster comprises 281 installations present in the European Union Transaction Log every year between 2005 and 2023. After requiring positive verified emissions and matched Annual Accounts revenue, the analysis sample covers 88\% of total Belgian ETS emissions in 2005 (162--194 firms per year).

\subsection{The Market Stability Reserve as the identifying event}\label{sec:msr_identification}

Identification relies on a sharp pre/post contrast around the 2015 Market Stability Reserve (MSR) reform. Decision (EU) 2015/1814 of October 2015 established a rules-based mechanism that automatically removes a fixed fraction of allowances from circulation whenever the Total Number of Allowances in Circulation exceeds 833 million tons \citep{Perino2018}. The rule is mechanical and pre-specified, so the post-2018 EUA price path is determined by accumulated surplus and the MSR's intake schedule, not by contemporaneous economic conditions. The reform triggered a tenfold rise in EUA prices: from $\sim$€6--8 per tCO$_2$ in 2015--16 to above €80 in 2022. Cross-firm variation in exposure is determined by pre-MSR firm characteristics (emission intensity, free-allowance allocation), and our treatment intensity below is constructed from data ending in 2015. \citet{kanzig2023unequal}'s 114-event high-frequency carbon-policy-surprise series and \citet{BauerKaenzigRudebusch2026}'s extension to 2024 confirm that the post-2015 EUA path is policy-driven (first-stage F~$> 16$). We use 2015 as the cutoff for ``Post'' periods and report event-study coefficients separately for each post-2015 calendar year. Section~\ref{sec:domestic_phase2} presents an alternative event-study using the 2008 Phase~II start as the cutoff with treatment intensity built on 2005--2008 data.

\subsection{Belgian B2B firm-to-firm transactions}

The National Bank of Belgium maintains a near-universal panel of B2B transactions, derived from VAT-listing reports that all Belgian firms above a low size threshold submit annually. Each record is a (seller VAT, buyer VAT, year, EUR amount) tuple. The panel covers 2002--2023 and includes domestic transactions only. We use 2005--2022. We restrict to private-sector buyers and sellers with a primary NACE 4-digit code and merge in Annual Accounts (revenue, total cost, employment) and EUTL records (verified emissions, free allowances) at the firm $\times$ year level.

\subsection{Belgian customs panel}\label{sec:data_customs}

For the across-country test (\S\ref{sec:international}) we use the Belgian customs panel maintained by the NBB and Eurostat, at the firm $\times$ HS6 product $\times$ source country $\times$ year level. The headline CMdG-replication specification uses 2000--2019 to match \citet{CosterMejeanDiGiovanni2024} (CMdG hereafter). The buyer-supplier-level customs analysis (\S\ref{sec:international_buyer_supplier} onward) extends through 2022 to align with the within-country B2B sample window and capture the post-2017 EUA price rise. We follow CMdG in classifying HS6 products as regulated/unregulated via concordance to EUTL installation activity codes; for the headline replication only, we restrict the sample to imports from non-ETS source countries (per CMdG's identifying choice). The buyer-supplier extension keeps both EU and non-ETS source rows.

\subsection{Treatment intensity}

For each ETS supplier $j$ we construct a time-invariant pre-shock measure of carbon-cost intensity:
\begin{equation}
    \text{firm cost share}_j = \frac{\overline{\text{shortage}_{j,t}}_{\,t \in 2013..2016}\;\times\;\overline{\text{EUA}_t}_{\,t \in 2013..2016}}{\overline{\text{total cost}_{j,t}}_{\,t \in 2010..2012}},
    \label{eq:fcs}
\end{equation}
where $\text{shortage}_{j,t} = \max(0,\,\text{emissions}_{j,t} - \text{free allowances}_{j,t})$ in tCO$_2$, EUA$_t$ is the average annual allowance price, and total cost is from Annual Accounts. Both numerator and denominator end in 2016 or earlier, so the regressor cannot mechanically incorporate post-2015 outcomes. Cost-share intensity ranges from approximately zero (no shortage, or shortage covered by free allowances) to $\sim$5--10\% in the most-exposed installations. Within NACE 4-digit, after partialling out the sector mean, the cross-firm standard deviation is 0.037.

For the across-NACE-4d category test we aggregate to NACE 4-digit:
\begin{equation}
    \text{nace exposure}_n = \frac{\sum_{j \in \text{ETS sellers in } n} \overline{\text{corr sales}_{j,t}}_{\,t \in 2010..14} \times \text{firm cost share}_j}{\sum_{j \in \text{all sellers in } n} \overline{\text{corr sales}_{j,t}}_{\,t \in 2010..14}},
    \label{eq:nace_exposure}
\end{equation}
a sales-weighted average ETS exposure within the supplier sector, set to zero where no ETS sellers are active.

For the Phase~II alternative event-study (\S\ref{sec:domestic_phase2}), we re-construct equation~\eqref{eq:fcs} on the 2005--2008 numerator window with a 2003--2005 cost denominator (or earliest available three-year window), so the regressor remains predetermined relative to the 2008 cutoff.
