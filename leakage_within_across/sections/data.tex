\subsection{The EU ETS in Belgium}

The EU Emissions Trading System (EU ETS) is a carbon-pricing policy that sets a continent-wide cap on annual emissions, distributes allowances summing to the cap, and lets regulated firms trade them on a secondary market. A regulated installation must surrender one allowance for each tonne of CO\textsubscript{2} it emits within the year; scarcity of allowances commands a positive price.

Coverage is at the installation level and is determined by activity and size. The system covers electricity and heat generation and a list of energy-intensive manufacturing activities --- refining, iron and steel, cement, lime, glass, ceramics, paper, basic chemicals, and a handful of others --- but within each covered activity only installations above a minimum-capacity threshold are included (for example, 20~MW of rated thermal input for combustion installations). We link Belgian installations to their parent firm via VAT identifier. The Belgian roster includes 281 installations active in the EU Transaction Log every year between 2005 and 2023; after requiring positive verified emissions and matched Annual Accounts revenue, the analysis sample covers 88\% of total Belgian ETS emissions in 2005, with 162--194 firms per year.

The system has operated in four trading phases since 2005. Phase~I (2005--2007) was a pilot phase: allowances were allocated for free under National Allocation Plans, and the May~2006 release of verified emissions revealed substantial over-allocation. Phase~II (2008--2012) tightened the cap and introduced limited auctioning, but the 2008 financial crisis depressed industrial emissions and produced a structural surplus. Phase~III (2013--2020) made auctioning the default allocation method for electricity generation and introduced sector-specific best-available-technology benchmarks for free allocation in industry; a ``carbon-leakage list'' identified sectors deemed at significant risk of cross-border substitution and granted them continued full free allocation. Phase~IV (2021--) tightened the cap further and began phasing out free allocation for sectors not on the leakage list. \subsection{Belgian B2B firm-to-firm transactions}

The National Bank of Belgium maintains a near-universal panel of B2B transactions, derived from VAT-listing reports that all Belgian firms above a low size threshold submit annually. Each record is a (seller VAT, buyer VAT, year, EUR amount) tuple. The panel covers 2002--2023 and includes domestic transactions only. We use 2005--2022. We restrict to private-sector buyers and sellers with a primary NACE 4-digit code and merge in Annual Accounts (revenue, total cost, employment) at the firm $\times$ year level.

\subsection{Belgian customs panel}\label{sec:data_customs}

For the international margin (\S\ref{sec:international}) we use the Belgian customs panel maintained by the NBB and Eurostat, at the firm $\times$ HS6 product $\times$ source country $\times$ year level. The headline \citet{CosterMejeanDiGiovanni2024} (CdGM hereafter) replication uses 2000--2019 to match their window. The buyer-supplier-level customs analysis (\S\ref{sec:international}) extends through 2022 to align with the within-country B2B sample window and to capture the post-2017 EUA price rise. We follow CdGM in classifying HS6 products as regulated or unregulated via concordance to EUTL installation activity codes.

\subsection{The EU Transaction Log: emissions and allowance allocation}\label{sec:data_eutl}

The European Union Transaction Log records, for each regulated installation and year, the verified emissions and the free allowances allocated under the EU ETS. We link each EUTL installation to its parent firm via Belgian VAT identifiers and merge the resulting firm-year panel with the Annual Accounts. The headline firm-year quantity we extract is the \emph{shortage}:
\begin{equation}
    \text{shortage}_{j,t} \;=\; \max\!\left(0,\; \text{emissions}_{j,t} - \text{free allowances}_{j,t}\right),
    \label{eq:shortage}
\end{equation}
the tonnes of CO\textsubscript{2} for which firm $j$ in year $t$ had to either buy allowances at auction, purchase them on the secondary market, or pay the EU ETS non-compliance penalty. Firms with surplus allocation in a given year have $\text{shortage}_{j,t} = 0$ and face no marginal cost from the EU ETS that year.

To put the shortage on a scale comparable across firms of different sizes, we divide the shortage--EUA cost by the firm's total operating cost from the Annual Accounts. The resulting carbon cost share,
\[
    \omega_{j,t} \;=\; \frac{\text{shortage}_{j,t} \cdot \text{EUA}_t}{\text{total cost}_{j,t}},
\]
is the fraction of firm $j$'s operating cost attributable to allowance payments in year $t$. Table~\ref{tab:firm_cost_share_by_phase} reports its distribution across the four ETS trading phases.

The phase pattern is sharp. In Phase~I and Phase~II, free allocation generously covered ETS firms' emissions: the median firm has zero shortage in both phases, and even the 99th percentile of $\omega$ stays below half a percent of total cost. Phase~III pre-MSR begins to bite in the right tail (p99 = 8.7\%) but the median firm still has near-zero shortage. The MSR-driven price rise pushes p90 to 1.4\% post-MSR and to 2.6\% in Phase~IV, with the right tail reaching 60\% of total cost at the most-exposed installations. A complementary metric --- the effective carbon price per tonne emitted, computed as the ratio of total carbon cost paid to total emissions --- rises from \$1.80/tCO\textsubscript{2} in Phase~I to \$32.82/tCO\textsubscript{2} in Phase~IV, an eighteen-fold increase. Phase~IV is the only phase in the policy's history in which the carbon cost share is unambiguously non-trivial at the population level.
