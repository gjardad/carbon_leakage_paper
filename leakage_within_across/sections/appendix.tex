\section{Variable definitions}\label{app:variables}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{shortage} (\eqref{eq:shortage}): firm-year allowance shortfall in tCO\textsubscript{2}, $\max(0, \text{emissions}_{j,t} - \text{free allowances}_{j,t})$.
    \item \textbf{carbon cost share} $\omega_{j,t}$: shortage times EUA price, divided by the firm's total operating cost in year $t$. Reported by phase in Table~\ref{tab:firm_cost_share_by_phase}.
    \item \textbf{Post}: indicator for years on or after the relevant ETS event year ($\tau \in \{2006, 2017\}$ in the within-NACE-4d specification, corresponding to the EU ETS launch in 2005 and the MSR-driven price rise from 2017).
    \item \textbf{regulated}: indicator that the NACE 4-digit category contains at least one ETS-regulated installation.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Rambachan--Roth sensitivity for column 4}\label{app:passthrough_rr}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------

The headline event-study figure \ref{fig:passthrough_eventstudy} reports $\hat\beta_\tau$ for $X_s = \omega^{sh}_s$ year by year, with 2016 as the omitted reference. The differential turns on at 2017 and rises through 2022. We test how robust the first post-treatment coefficient $\hat\beta_{2017}$ is to a relaxation of the parallel-trends assumption, following \citet{rambachanroth2023}.

We report two sets of bounds. The relative-magnitudes restriction $\Delta^{RM}(\bar M)$ asks: if the post-treatment violation of parallel trends is at most $\bar M$ times the largest pre-treatment violation, what is the bias-robust confidence set for $\hat\beta_{2017}$? The smoothness restriction $\Delta^{SD}(M)$ asks: if the second differences of the post-treatment trend are bounded by $M$, what is the FLCI?

\section{Relational capital: new-pair omega-rank falsification}\label{app:omega_rank}

This appendix details the empirical test of mechanism 3 in §\ref{sec:discussion} (relational capital). The argument runs as follows. The existing-portfolio margin tests in §\ref{sec:domestic_within_intensive} and §\ref{sec:domestic_across} show no within-NACE 4-digit substitution among Belgian buyers after the MSR decision. One candidate explanation is that switching costs --- accumulated trust, contract-specific information, sunk relationship-specific investments --- prevent substitution even when relative prices change. If that is the binding friction, it should not bind on the new-relationship margin: a buyer forming a fresh supplier pair starts from a clean slate. Buyers forming new pairs in an ETS-treated NACE 4-digit sector after the MSR decision should therefore pick systematically cleaner suppliers than buyers forming new pairs before. This appendix tests that prediction directly and reports a null at $\tau = 2017$.

% ----------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Setup and identification independence from existing-portfolio tests}\label{app:omega_rank_setup}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------

Define a "new pair" as a (buyer, supplier) pair first observed in the Belgian B2B panel in a given year. Restrict to suppliers in ETS-treated NACE 4-digit sectors with a non-missing within-NACE 4-digit allowance-shortage rank: $\omega_{j,t} = \text{shortage}_{j,t} / \text{total cost}_{j,t}$, then percentile-rank within (NACE 4-digit, year) among all EUTL firms with non-missing $\omega$. Higher rank $=$ more carbon-cost-exposed.

Identification at the new-pair margin is essentially disjoint from the existing-portfolio tests in the main text. Only 4--15\% of new omega-matched pairs per year land in a buyer-NACE 4-digit-year cell that has $\geq 2$ omega-able suppliers; the remaining 85--96\% are single-seller cells, invisible to the within-cell intensive-margin tests in §\ref{sec:domestic_within_intensive} by construction. For omega-matched new pairs, the median share of the new supplier within the buyer's NACE 4-digit spend is $100\%$ throughout the 2006--2022 panel. The new-pair margin captures market entry; the within-cell tests cover portfolio rebalancing within already-established sourcing. The two margins share no identifying variation.

% ----------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{\texorpdfstring{Headline: year-$t$-rank event study on rank-stable NACE 4-digit sectors}{Headline: year-t-rank event study on rank-stable NACE 4-digit sectors}}\label{app:omega_rank_headline}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------

The natural test is the year-$t$-rank event study: for new pairs formed in year $t$, what is the supplier's within-NACE 4-digit rank at the time of pair formation? If buyers shift toward cleaner suppliers post-MSR, the mean rank of new picks should fall.

A direct year-$t$-rank event study on the full sample is contaminated, because within-NACE 4-digit ranks shift across years for reasons unrelated to buyer choice (Appendix~\ref{app:omega_rank_rank_instability} below documents the channels). To isolate buyer choice, we restrict the sample to NACE 4-digit sectors where the supplier rank ordering is stable across the post-event window. For each NACE 4-digit sector with $\geq 3$ EUTL firms tracked at both 2016 and 2022, we compute the Spearman rank correlation $\rho$ between supplier ranks at the two endpoints. "Stable" sectors are those with $\rho$ above a threshold.

At $\tau = 2017$, 17 NACE 4-digit sectors have $\geq 3$ EUTL firms tracked at both endpoints. Of these, $7$ have $\rho \geq 0.8$ ($28$ firms, $\sim 1\,030$ new pairs in the 2012--2022 window) and $5$ have $\rho \geq 0.9$ ($18$ firms, $\sim 950$ new pairs). We report both as parallel specifications. At $\tau = 2005$ no NACE 4-digit sector is rank-stable at any reasonable threshold (the within-NACE 4-digit rank distribution scrambles almost completely across Phase~I; see Appendix~\ref{app:omega_rank_rank_instability}), so the stable-sector test cannot be run there, and $\tau = 2017$ stands as the headline event.

Within each stable subsample we estimate the event-study
\begin{equation}
    \text{rank}^{yt}_{ij} \;=\; \sum_{\substack{e = -4 \\ e \neq -1}}^{+4} \beta_e \cdot \mathbf{1}\big\{\text{year\_first}_{ij} - 2017 = e\big\} \;+\; \alpha_b \;+\; \gamma_n \;+\; \varepsilon_{ij},
    \label{eq:omega_rank_eventstudy}
\end{equation}
where $\text{rank}^{yt}_{ij}$ is the supplier's within-NACE 4-digit percentile rank in $\text{year\_first}_{ij}$ (the year pair $ij$ is first observed), $\alpha_b$ is a buyer fixed effect, $\gamma_n$ is a supplier-NACE 4-digit fixed effect, and standard errors are two-way clustered on buyer and supplier-NACE 4-digit. The omitted reference category is rel-year $= -1$ (year\_first $= 2016$).

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{phase4_new_relationships_omega_rank_supplier_test_stable_yeartrank_event_study.pdf}
    \caption{Event-study coefficients $\hat\beta_e$ from \eqref{eq:omega_rank_eventstudy} at $\tau = 2017$, restricted to NACE 4-digit sectors where the Spearman rank correlation $\rho$ between supplier ranks at 2016 and 2022 exceeds the legend threshold ($\rho \geq 0.8$: 7 sectors, 28 firms; $\rho \geq 0.9$: 5 sectors, 18 firms). Bars are $95\%$ confidence intervals from two-way-clustered (buyer, supplier-NACE 4-digit) standard errors. The omitted reference is rel-year $= -1$ (year\_first $= 2016$). Vertical dashed line at rel-year $= -0.5$ marks the event boundary. Source: \texttt{phase4\_new\_relationships\_omega\_rank\_supplier\_test.R}.}
    \label{fig:omega_rank_stable_yeartrank}
\end{figure}

The figure does not display a clean post-event drift. Pre-period coefficients at rel-year $= -4$ and $-3$ sit at $\pm 0.05$, but rel-year $= -2$ (year\_first $= 2015$) spikes to $+0.11$ at $\rho \geq 0.9$ and $+0.06$ at $\rho \geq 0.8$. The first post-event coefficient at rel-year $= 0$ (year\_first $= 2017$) sits at $+0.087$ across both thresholds. From rel-year $= +1$ onward, coefficients hover within $\pm 0.05$ of zero, with $95\%$ confidence intervals that include zero at every horizon.

We read the rel-year $= -2$ and rel-year $= 0$ spikes as artifacts of the 2015--2016 B2B reporting-threshold change (Appendix~\ref{app:omega_rank_cohort_diagnostic}): the 2016 reference cohort is an unusually large influx of long-tenured suppliers whose within-sector ranks happen to be low, so years adjacent to 2016 mechanically appear elevated relative to that reference. The symmetric spike pattern at rel-year $= -2$ (one year before the reporting wave) and rel-year $= 0$ (one year after) is consistent with this interpretation and inconsistent with a behavioral post-MSR drift.

The defensible reading: from rel-year $= +1$ onward, post-event year-$t$-rank coefficients are bounded within $\pm 0.05$ of zero. Buyers forming new supplier pairs more than one year after the MSR decision do not select systematically cleaner suppliers than their 2016 counterparts. The sample is thin ($28$ firms in $7$ sectors at the headline threshold), so the result is "absence of evidence" rather than "evidence of absence": we cannot reject moderate effects in either direction, but find no sustained signal in the direction relational capital would predict.

% ----------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Why restrict to stable-rank NACE 4-digit sectors?}\label{app:omega_rank_rank_instability}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------

The stable-sector restriction in the headline test is needed because the within-NACE 4-digit rank distribution is unstable at the firm level across the post-event window. Two pieces of evidence.

\paragraph{Rank mean-reversion.} For the $120$ EUTL firms with non-missing rank in both 2016 and 2022, regressing $\Delta \text{rank}_i = \text{rank}_{i, 2022} - \text{rank}_{i, 2016}$ on $\text{rank}_{i, 2016}$ within sector gives a slope of $-0.536$ (standard error $0.115$, $p < 0.001$, clustered at the NACE 4-digit level). High-rank firms in 2016 systematically lose rank by 2022. Equivalently, rank persistence is $\rho \approx 0.46$ over the six-year window. At $\tau = 2005$ the mean-reversion is even stronger: the $89$ EUTL firms tracked in both 2005 and 2010 give a slope of $-0.946$ ($p < 0.001$), implying near-zero rank persistence over Phase~I. This is why no NACE 4-digit sector is rank-stable at $\tau = 2005$.

Mean-reversion in rank, combined with the descriptive fact that buyers slightly prefer above-median-rank suppliers (mean pick rank $\approx 0.55$, above the random-pick value of $0.5$), produces a mechanical decline in mean year-$t$ pick rank of roughly $(0.55 - 0.50) \times (1 - \rho) \approx 0.027$ over the window --- of similar order to the $\sim 0.05$ decline visible in the unrestricted descriptive year-$t$ trajectory. Without a sample restriction that removes this mechanical channel, a year-$t$-rank decline cannot be cleanly attributed to buyer choice.

\paragraph{Rank-change decomposition.} The carbon-cost rank composite $\omega_{j,t} = (\text{emissions}_{j,t} - \text{free allowances}_{j,t}) / \text{total cost}_{j,t}$ can shift because emissions, free allocations, or total cost change. Regressing $\Delta \log X_j$ on $\text{rank}_{j, \text{ref}}$ within sector for each of $X \in \{\text{emissions, free allocations, total cost, emissions/cost}\}$ identifies which channels drive the mean-reversion.

Three observations.

First, free allocations are unrelated to pre-event rank at both events ($\hat\beta_{\text{alloc}} \approx 0$, $p > 0.65$). The rank decline of high-rank firms is not a mechanical policy artifact via allocation revisions.

Second, at $\tau = 2017$ emissions decline strongly with pre-event rank ($\hat\beta_{\text{em}} = -0.423$, $p = 0.02$) while costs do not ($\hat\beta_{\text{cost}} = -0.186$, $p = 0.51$). The emissions-intensity coefficient $\hat\beta_{\text{em/cost}} = -0.229$ leans negative without reaching significance. The pattern is consistent with high-rank firms reducing emissions through real intensity-reducing abatement, not purely output shrinkage.

Third, at $\tau = 2005$ emissions and costs decline together (both at $p \approx 0.06$), and the intensity coefficient is not separately identifiable. The Phase~I rank-decline of high-rank firms looks like output shrinkage; we cannot pin down a residual abatement channel.

The combined reading: across both events, the supplier-side rank decline among high-rank firms reflects a behavioral channel --- real abatement at $\tau = 2017$, output shrinkage and possibly abatement at $\tau = 2005$. Using year-$t$ rank as the LHS on the full sample would conflate this supplier-side behavior with the buyer-side variation we want to identify. The stable-rank sector restriction in the headline test eliminates the channel by construction: within sectors where the rank ordering is preserved, the supplier's year-$t$ rank equals their rank at the reference year, and the LHS captures only buyer choice.

\subsection{Supplementary: supplier-level Story-A-vs-B DiD}\label{app:omega_rank_supplier_did}
% ----------------------------------------------------------------

We complement the headline event study with a supplier-level DiD on new-buyer counts. The test isolates a different version of the behavioral question: do suppliers who abated more (lost more rank) gain disproportionately more new buyers post-event? If buyers' choice rule shifted toward cleaner suppliers, abater suppliers should attract more new pairs after the policy than non-abater suppliers.

The unit is (supplier $\times$ period) with $\text{period} \in \{\text{pre, post}\}$ around the event. For $\tau = 2017$, the pre period is 2012--2016 and the post period is 2017--2022:
\begin{equation}
    \log\big(1 + n^{\text{pairs}}_{it}\big) \;=\; \beta \cdot \text{post}_t \cdot \text{abater}_i \;+\; \alpha_i \;+\; \gamma_t \;+\; \varepsilon_{it},
    \label{eq:omega_rank_supplier_did}
\end{equation}
where $n^{\text{pairs}}_{it}$ counts new buyer relationships supplier $i$ forms in period $t$ and $\text{abater}_i = \mathbf{1}\{\Delta \text{rank}_i < \text{median}(\Delta \text{rank})\}$ flags suppliers whose 2016-to-2022 rank decline exceeds the cross-supplier median. Standard errors are clustered at the supplier. $\beta > 0$ would indicate a buyer-side shift toward abaters.
