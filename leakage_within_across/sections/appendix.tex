\section{Variable definitions}\label{app:variables}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{firm cost share} (\eqref{eq:fcs}): pre-shock firm-level carbon cost share, time-invariant. Numerator: 2013--2016 average annual allowance shortfall in tCO$_2$ priced at the year-specific EUA. Denominator: 2010--2012 average total operating cost from Annual Accounts. Falls back to the earliest available three-year window for firms without 2010--2012 data.
    \item \textbf{nace exposure} (\eqref{eq:nace_exposure}): sales-weighted average firm cost share within a NACE 4-digit category, time-invariant; equals zero for categories with no ETS sellers.
    \item \textbf{share top}: annual share of the most-exposed ETS supplier $j^*(b,n)$ in cell $(b,n)$'s total spending in that NACE 4-digit category.
    \item \textbf{pair exposure}: $\text{firm cost share}_{j^*(b,n)} \times \bar{s}_{j^*(b,n)}$, where $\bar{s}_{j^*(b,n)} = \overline{\text{spending from } b \text{ to } j^*}_{2010..2014} / \overline{\text{total inputs of } b}_{2010..2014}$. Units: percentage of buyer's total input bill at risk if $j^*$'s carbon shock fully passes through and the buyer does not substitute.
    \item \textbf{Post}: $\mathbf{1}[t \geq 2015]$.
    \item \textbf{regulated}: $\mathbf{1}[\text{nace exposure}_n > 0]$.
\end{itemize}

\section{Pass-through: LP-IV invertibility-robust robustness}\label{app:passthrough_lp_iv}

The headline pass-through specification (\eqref{eq:passthrough_strategy1}) uses Käenzig's VAR-identified Shock as the regressor in a panel local projection. Identification of the Shock from \citet{kanzig2023unequal}'s external-instruments SVAR rests on (partial) invertibility. We follow \citet[Appendix C.5]{kanzig2023unequal} in supplementing the headline with a local-projection instrumental-variables (LP-IV) exercise that does not require invertibility, using the raw refined Surprise series as the instrument:
\begin{equation}
    \log P_{s, m+h} - \log P_{s, m-1} \;=\; \theta_h \cdot \omega_{s, t(m)} \cdot \big(\log p^{\text{EUA}}_m - \log p^{\text{EUA}}_{m-1}\big) \;+\; \sum_{j=1}^{6} \phi_{h,j} \log P_{s, m-j} \;+\; \alpha_s \;+\; \delta_m \;+\; \xi_{s,m,h}
    \label{eq:passthrough_lp_iv}
\end{equation}
estimated by 2SLS, with $\omega_{s, t(m)} \cdot \text{CPSurprise}_m$ instrumenting for $\omega_{s, t(m)} \cdot \Delta \log p^{\text{EUA}}_m$. As prescribed by \citet{kanzig2023unequal}, the impulse horizon is restricted to $h \leq 12$ months because the LP-IV signal-to-noise ratio at longer horizons is too low to credibly identify the carbon-policy effect against the myriad other shocks affecting EUA prices. Inference uses cluster-robust standard errors at NACE 4-digit, with lag-augmentation as in \citet{kanzig2023unequal}'s appendix specification.

\paragraph{Reading.} Confidence intervals are wide enough to include zero at every horizon, which matches the pattern \citet{kanzig2023unequal} reports for the LP-IV variant of his own HICP IRF (his Figure C.20). The point estimates do not contradict the headline panel-LP IRF — the headline's $\widehat{\gamma}_h$ at horizons up to $h = 12$ lies within the 95\% LP-IV band — but the LP-IV is too underpowered on the Belgian panel to deliver an independent point estimate. As Käenzig notes, this is a known limitation of LP-IV with high-frequency policy surprises against macroeconomic outcomes: the surprise series captures only the regulatory-news component of EUA-price variation, and at the monthly frequency this component is small relative to the cumulative effect of other (gas-market, demand, weather) shocks driving EUA. The robustness exhibit confirms that the headline conclusion does not rest on the SVAR's invertibility assumption in a way that LP-IV would reject.
