Carbon pricing is the climate-policy instrument that economists most consistently endorse, because it taxes the emissions externality directly and lets every emitter make its own decision about whether to abate or pay. But the regulators that impose carbon prices in practice are also concerned about the costs to their own producers. This concern has shaped the design of every major carbon-pricing scheme: each has been built with a set of protections aimed at sheltering regulated firms from competition with less-regulated firms abroad.\footnote{For example, this is the main motivation behind the free allocation of EU emission allowances in the early phases of the European Union Emissions Trading System (EU ETS), as well as the Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM), which has been phased in since 2023 as some sectors initially shielded from full auctioning are being incorporated.} The premise behind these protections is the same. Without them, regulated firms would lose business to firms that are not, and the climate benefit of the regulation would be partially undone.

At its core, this is a question about how firms reorganize their supply chains when they face a relevant and permanent cost shock. The stakes are highest at the international border, where substitution toward foreign suppliers means losing national output, and no government welcomes that. But the same question can be asked within a country. Do firms more exposed to the regulation lose output share to firms less exposed within the same economy? This domestic version matters in its own right, because carbon pricing in practice is incomplete. It covers some sectors and not others, and within covered sectors it covers some firms and not others. The aggregate climate effect of the policy therefore depends not only on how much regulated firms abate their emissions, but also on whether and how much output reallocates across firms differently affected by the regulation.

This paper measures both margins jointly in the context of the EU Emissions Trading System in Belgium. Domestically, we ask whether buyers shift purchases from more-exposed regulated firms to less-exposed firms within the same narrowly defined sector, and whether they shift expenditure across sectors away from regulated sectors toward less-regulated ones. Internationally, we ask whether, within narrowly defined product categories, Belgian importers shift purchases from suppliers inside the EU Emissions Trading System toward suppliers in countries that do not face the carbon price.

The Belgian data offer three rare advantages. The National Bank of Belgium maintains a near-universal panel of firm-to-firm transactions, recording every domestic invoice between two firms over our sample period. The customs administration records each firm's imports by product and by source country. The European Union Transactions Log records each regulated installation's annual emissions and free-allowance allocation. We link the three. The identifying variation comes from the 2015 Market Stability Reserve reform of the EU Emissions Trading System --- a credible commitment to tighten the carbon market over the following decade, driven by EU-wide political negotiations and plausibly exogenous to Belgian firm-level conditions --- which caused the allowance price to rise from around \EUR{7} per tonne in 2014 to peaks above \EUR{90} in 2022. \notrun{} We find no reallocation. The most-exposed regulated supplier in a buyer's input mix does not lose share to less-exposed regulated competitors in the same product market. Buyers do not shift expenditure away from the sectors most affected by the regulation. The share of regulated imports sourced from EU countries does not fall after 2015, in contrast to the French finding of substantial substitution toward non-EU sources in \citet{CosterMejeanDiGiovanni2024}. The null holds among the buyers most exposed to the policy at the level of their input bill (where the cost shock is largest), on both the intensive margin (the share of expenditure) and the extensive margin (whether the buyer sources from the regulated supplier at all), and around three separate policy-tightening events that span the life of the EU ETS.

These results are hard to reconcile with the rest of what we observe in the data. The carbon price did translate into prices for regulated producers. A standard structural carbon-policy shock identified by \citet{kanzig2023unequal} loads onto the Belgian producer price index with a peak coefficient of $+4.08$ at twelve months, and a regulated-versus-unregulated price-index event study with sector-specific trends recovers differential price gaps of roughly nine to eleven percentage points across the post-2008 period. The cost shock at the level of individual buyer-supplier relationships is also large in the regulated sectors most exposed to the policy --- cement, basic metals, and refining --- where the carbon cost reaches a meaningful share of the buyer's input bill. Prices rose. Buyers paid them. Neither side reorganized along any of the margins we can measure.

We propose five explanations for this joint pattern and assess the evidence on each. The most natural --- that the cost shock at the typical buyer's input bill is too small to overcome any reasonable cost of switching suppliers --- is consistent with the data on average but is harder to defend among the most exposed buyers, where we still find no reallocation. We rule out one mechanism directly: that long-standing incumbency in the regulated supplier relationship is the binding friction. If long-tenured incumbents were sheltered by the relationship itself, the post-2015 share loss for the most-exposed regulated supplier would be smallest where that supplier is also the buyer's longest-tenured incumbent. The pattern is the opposite. Three other candidates remain consistent with the data we have, and discriminating among them requires data we do not: that competing suppliers move prices together and so compress the relative-price signal that would induce buyer substitution, that pass-through from the carbon shock onto regulated suppliers' prices is only partial, and that the ``unregulated'' alternatives are themselves partially regulated through their own upstream regulated inputs.

We contribute to two strands of the literature. The first is the cross-border leakage literature, which has measured the response of import flows to unilateral environmental regulation using aggregate trade data \citep{aichelefelbermayr2015,naegelezaklan2019,satodechezlepretre2015} and, most recently, at the firm level using French customs data \citep{CosterMejeanDiGiovanni2024}. We test for cross-border leakage on Belgian customs data at the same firm-level granularity used in the French study, on an identification window that extends through the post-MSR high-price period that the French sample does not cover. Granular firm-level data can in principle reveal leakage that aggregate flows would miss, and the post-MSR high-price period can in principle reveal leakage that an early-Phase-III window would miss. Neither does in Belgium. The second is the firm-level literature on within-country EU ETS effects \citep{martin2014,dechezlepretreetal2023,colmeretal2023,kanzig2023unequal}, which documents that regulated firms reduced emissions without contracting output relative to unregulated controls. This literature has used the average effect on regulated firms; it has not exploited firm-level heterogeneity in carbon-price exposure within the regulated group. A firm-level null on the average treated effect is silent on whether more-exposed regulated firms lose share to less-exposed regulated firms in the same product market. That margin --- within the regulated group, between firms heterogeneous in exposure --- is where reallocation should be hardest to miss if it is operating. We test it directly and find a null there as well. We also focus on the buyer's response rather than the regulated firm's, a margin where reorganization could in principle appear even when the regulated firms themselves do not visibly contract.
