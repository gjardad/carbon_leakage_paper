European regulators have spent two decades acting as if the answer is yes. The EU's carbon market gives away more than half of its allowances for free, on the premise that without them regulated firms would lose business to less-regulated competitors. The Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism, phased in from 2023, taxes imports of steel, aluminum, cement, fertilizer, hydrogen, and electricity on the same premise. Both policies cost or distort something --- forgone revenue, sector-specific carve-outs, trade-policy risk --- and both rest on a quantitative claim about reallocation that has never been tested directly on firm-to-firm data. This paper tests it.

\paragraph{The question.} Does carbon pricing reallocate economic activity away from the firms it targets? The reallocation can take three forms. It can flow across-border to less-regulated jurisdictions --- the canonical leakage concern that motivates CBAM. It can flow domestically across firms in the same product market, since carbon pricing in practice has incomplete sectoral coverage within a jurisdiction. And it can flow across input categories within a buyer, as buyers tilt their input mix away from carbon-priced inputs. The first margin has been studied at the firm level by \citet{CosterMejeanDiGiovanni2024} on French customs data. The second and third have been inferred only indirectly from the fact that regulated EU ETS firms reduced emissions without contracting output \citep{martin2014,colmeretal2023,dechezlepretreetal2023,kanzig2023unequal}. None has been measured at the firm-to-firm level on a panel that links emission regulation to upstream and downstream transactions on the same economy.

\paragraph{The setting.} The National Bank of Belgium maintains a near-universal panel of business-to-business invoices linked by VAT identifier, the EU customs panel at the firm $\times$ HS6 product $\times$ source country $\times$ year level, and the EU Transactions Log of regulated installations. We link all three. The result is the first dataset that follows a Belgian firm's regulation status, its emissions and free-allocation trajectory, the suppliers it buys from at home and abroad, and the prices it pays --- over 2005--2022.

\paragraph{Before the question can be asked.} A null reallocation result is informative only if the shock had a chance to reorganize anything. Section~\ref{sec:shock_magnitude} establishes that it did. EUA prices rose roughly tenfold over the post-MSR period, from $\approx$\EUR{7}/tCO\textsubscript{2} in 2014 to peaks above \EUR{90} in 2022, and the resulting cost shock is large for a non-trivial subset of buyer-supplier relationships --- concentrated where allocation tightened and emission intensity is high. Section~\ref{sec:passthrough} establishes that this cost shock translated into prices: aggregate \citet{kanzig2023unequal}-style pass-through onto the Belgian PPI peaks at $+4.08$ (s.e.\ $0.64$) at horizon 12 months, and a regulated-vs-unregulated event study with sector-specific linear trends recovers Phase~II and Phase~IV differential PPI gaps of $+0.094$ and $+0.113$. Prices rose. The question of whether buyers reorganized in response is well-posed.

\paragraph{Findings.} On all three reallocation margins, the answer is no. \notrun{} Within NACE 4-digit, the most-exposed regulated supplier does not lose share to less-exposed competitors after 2015: $\widehat{\beta} = X$ (s.e.\ $X$). Across NACE 4-digit, buyers do not shift expenditure away from regulated input categories: $\widehat{\beta} = X$ (s.e.\ $X$). Across international sources within HS6, the share of regulated imports sourced from EU partners is essentially flat through 2022 \citep[contrasting with][on French data]{CosterMejeanDiGiovanni2024}; the visible cross-border substitution in the customs panel is in \emph{unregulated} products, which is wrong-signed for a carbon-policy mechanism. Prices rose; buyers paid them; neither side reorganized.

\paragraph{Contributions.} We make three contributions to the EU ETS reallocation literature.

\emph{First,} we measure reallocation \emph{within} the treated group, exploiting continuous heterogeneity in firm-level exposure. Existing firm-level designs compare regulated to unregulated firms and pool treated firms together. The average treated effect is silent on whether more-exposed treated firms lose share to less-exposed treated firms. We test this directly. Two firms in the same NACE 4-digit, both ETS-regulated but with different emission intensity and different free-allocation tightness, face different effective shocks. If the policy reallocates among the firms it bites hardest, this is the design that should find it. It does not.

\emph{Second,} we identify in the high-price period of the EU ETS. Prior null results in the firm-level literature cover Phase~II and early Phase~III, when EUA prices averaged below \EUR{10}/tCO\textsubscript{2} and the policy was plausibly not biting enough to reorganize production. Our sample covers the post-MSR run-up and Phase~IV, where prices peaked above \EUR{90}. A null in this period is a harder test of the leakage story than the literature has been able to run before.

\emph{Third,} we measure the domestic and international margins on the same economy in the same identification window. The leakage literature has measured the two margins separately, with different evidentiary bases; \citet{CosterMejeanDiGiovanni2024} on French firm-level customs is the closest benchmark on the international side, and the within-country margin has been inferred only indirectly. Whether the two margins move together or diverge is itself an empirical question. In Belgium they move together: both produce null. Whether this carries to other economies is open.

\paragraph{Why no reallocation?} The natural first-pass explanation --- that the typical shock is too small for buyer substitution to be detectable --- is consistent with the data but is not the only candidate. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} evaluates five: shock-magnitude limits, strategic complementarities in pricing, relational capital in long-tenured buyer-supplier links, partial pass-through, and contamination of the unregulated control via the production network. We rule out one directly: relational capital. If long-tenured incumbency in the most-exposed regulated supplier were the binding friction, the post-2015 share loss would be smallest where the regulated supplier is also the longest-tenured incumbent. The pattern is the opposite. The other four candidates remain consistent with the data; the data we have do not separately identify them.

\paragraph{What we do not measure.} We measure three margins of reallocation: across domestic suppliers within a sector, across input categories within a buyer, and across international sources within an HS6 product. We do not measure: (i) production relocation, in which the regulated firm itself moves operations outside Belgium; (ii) services trade, since the customs panel covers goods only; (iii) imports of regulated services or non-HS6-classified products. We also do not estimate a structural model that decomposes aggregate emission responses into abatement and reallocation channels. The contribution is empirical: direct measurement on three margins where prior work has been indirect or one-sided.

\paragraph{Related literature.} We connect to four strands.

\emph{Cross-border leakage.} \citet{aichelefelbermayr2015}, \citet{naegelezaklan2019}, and \citet{satodechezlepretre2015} measure cross-border leakage via aggregate trade flows; \citet{CosterMejeanDiGiovanni2024} provides the firm-level analog on French customs data. Our cross-border test is the Belgian counterpart, on a panel that runs through 2022 and so captures the Phase~IV EUA peak that France's pre-2020 window misses.

\emph{Within-country EU ETS firm-level effects.} \citet{martin2014}, \citet{dechezlepretreetal2023}, \citet{colmeretal2023}, and \citet{kanzig2023unequal} document that EU ETS firms reduced emissions without contracting output relative to unregulated controls. The standard reading --- that within-country leakage must be small --- has not been tested directly on within-treated-group heterogeneity. We do so.

\emph{Production-network calibration.} \citet{baqaee2019}, \citet{king2019}, and \citet{aghion2024} provide the theoretical machinery for translating firm-level reallocation responses into aggregate-emissions effects of carbon policy. The elasticity of substitution $\sigma$ across regulated and unregulated suppliers is the central calibration target. Our reduced-form null is direct firm-level evidence on this elasticity.

\emph{Demand elasticity in environmental settings.} \citet{Hsiao2026} estimates own-price palm-oil demand elasticities of $0.7$ to $0.9$ and emphasizes that low substitution \emph{strengthens} the case for trade-policy intervention because it limits leakage to unregulated markets. The structural role of his elasticity is analogous to $\sigma$ in our production-network setting: low substitution turns a reduced-form null into a positive argument for unilateral environmental policy.
