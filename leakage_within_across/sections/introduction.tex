A unilateral carbon price covers some firms and not others. Standard theory predicts that the regulated firms will lose business to their unregulated competitors, raising emissions in the unregulated sector and partly offsetting the regulated reductions. The EU Emissions Trading System (EU ETS), the largest unilateral carbon-pricing scheme in operation, was designed around this prediction. Free allocation of more than half of EUA allowances, sectoral carbon-leakage lists, and the Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism phased in from 2023 are all policy responses to the reallocation that, without them, the regulation would presumably cause.

The empirical evidence on this prediction is mixed. Firm-level studies of the EU ETS find that regulated firms reduced emissions without contracting output relative to unregulated controls \citep{martin2014, colmeretal2023, dechezlepretreetal2023, kanzig2023unequal}. \citet{CosterMejeanDiGiovanni2024}, in contrast, find a substantial shift of French regulated-product imports toward non-EU sources after 2013. The two readings are not necessarily inconsistent: regulated firms could hold their own output steady while losing share of the domestic market to non-EU imports. But they have not been tested jointly on a single economy within a single identification window, and neither margin has been tested within the regulated group itself, using firm-level heterogeneity in carbon-price exposure.

This paper measures three margins of reallocation on Belgian firm-level data over 2005--2022. We link three administrative panels maintained by the National Bank of Belgium and by Eurostat: a near-universal panel of business-to-business invoices linked by VAT identifier, the EU customs panel at the firm $\times$ HS6 product $\times$ source country $\times$ year level, and the EU Transactions Log of regulated installations. The resulting dataset follows each Belgian firm's regulation status, emissions, free-allocation trajectory, supplier composition, and import sourcing through 2022.

The 2015 Market Stability Reserve reform supplies the policy event. EUA prices rose from approximately \EUR{7}/tCO\textsubscript{2} in 2014 to peaks above \EUR{90} in 2022 --- roughly a tenfold increase in five years, much larger than the variation prior leakage studies have used for identification. Section~\ref{sec:shock_magnitude} documents that the resulting cost shock is large at the level of individual buyer-supplier relationships in cement, basic metals, and refining. Section~\ref{sec:passthrough} documents that the shock translated into prices: the \citet{kanzig2023unequal} structural carbon-policy shock loads onto the Belgian PPI with a peak coefficient of $+4.08$ (s.e.\ $0.64$) at horizon 12 months, and a regulated-vs-unregulated event study with sector-specific linear trends recovers Phase~II and Phase~IV differential PPI gaps of $+0.094$ and $+0.113$.

We test three margins of reallocation. Within NACE 4-digit, we measure whether buyers shift purchases away from the most-exposed regulated supplier toward less-exposed competitors in the same product market. Across NACE 4-digit, we measure whether buyers shift expenditure away from regulated input categories toward unregulated ones within their own input bill. Within HS6 across source countries, we measure whether Belgian imports of regulated products shift from EU to non-EU sources.

On the international margin, the share of regulated imports from EU-ETS sources is essentially flat over 2000--2022, in contrast to the French result in \citet{CosterMejeanDiGiovanni2024}. The null holds in the top quartile of buyer-level exposure, where the shock bites hardest, and around three separate ETS event windows (Phase~II in 2008, Phase~III in 2013, and post-MSR in 2017). Prices rose; buyers paid them; neither side reorganized along any margin we can measure.

\paragraph{Contributions.} The paper extends three strands of the EU ETS reallocation literature. First, it measures reallocation \emph{within} the regulated group, using continuous heterogeneity in firm-level exposure to the carbon price. Existing firm-level designs compare regulated to unregulated firms and pool the regulated firms together; the average treated effect is silent on whether more-exposed regulated firms lose share to less-exposed regulated firms. Second, the identification window covers the post-MSR high-price period, when EUA prices rose roughly tenfold; prior null results in the firm-level literature mostly cover Phase~II and early Phase~III, when EUA prices averaged below \EUR{10}/tCO\textsubscript{2}. Third, the domestic and international margins are measured on the same economy in the same window. In Belgium they move together (both null), which extends the within-country firm-level result to the cross-border margin and stands in contrast to the French cross-border finding of \citet{CosterMejeanDiGiovanni2024}.

\paragraph{Mechanisms.} The natural first explanation for the joint null --- that the shock is too small at the typical buyer's input bill --- is consistent with the data but does not explain the absence of reallocation in the top quartile of buyer-level exposure, where the shock is largest. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} considers five candidate mechanisms and rules out one: relational capital. If long-tenured incumbency in the most-exposed regulated supplier were the binding friction, the post-2015 share loss would be smallest where the regulated supplier is also the longest-tenured incumbent. The pattern is the opposite. The other four candidates --- shock-magnitude limits, strategic complementarities in pricing, partial pass-through, and network-adjusted control-group contamination --- remain consistent with the data we have, and discriminating among them requires data we do not.

\paragraph{Scope.} We measure three margins of reallocation: across domestic suppliers within a NACE 4-digit, across input categories within a buyer, and across international sources within an HS6 product. We do not measure: (i) production relocation, in which the regulated firm itself moves operations outside Belgium; (ii) services trade, since the customs panel covers goods only; (iii) imports of regulated products that do not appear in the HS6 customs classification. We also do not estimate a structural model that decomposes the aggregate emission response into abatement and reallocation channels. The contribution is empirical: direct measurement on three reallocation margins for an economy and a period in which prior measurement has been partial or indirect.

\paragraph{Related literature.} We connect to four strands. \emph{Cross-border leakage}: \citet{aichelefelbermayr2015}, \citet{naegelezaklan2019}, and \citet{satodechezlepretre2015} measure cross-border leakage using aggregate trade flows; \citet{CosterMejeanDiGiovanni2024} provides the firm-level analog on French customs data. Our cross-border test is the Belgian counterpart, on a panel that runs through 2022 and so captures the Phase~IV EUA peak that French data through 2019 do not. \emph{Within-country EU ETS firm-level effects}: \citet{martin2014}, \citet{dechezlepretreetal2023}, \citet{colmeretal2023}, and \citet{kanzig2023unequal} document the firm-level null on output. The standard reading --- that within-country leakage must be small --- has not been tested directly on within-treated-group heterogeneity; we do so. \emph{Production-network calibration}: \citet{baqaee2019}, \citet{king2019}, and \citet{aghion2024} provide the theoretical machinery for translating firm-level reallocation responses into aggregate-emissions effects of carbon policy, and the elasticity of substitution across regulated and unregulated suppliers is the central calibration target. Our reduced-form null is direct firm-level evidence on this elasticity. \emph{Demand elasticity in environmental settings}: \citet{Hsiao2026} estimates own-price palm-oil demand elasticities of $0.7$ to $0.9$ and shows that low substitution \emph{strengthens} the case for trade-policy intervention by limiting leakage to unregulated markets. The structural role of his elasticity is analogous to the role of $\sigma$ in our production-network setting.
