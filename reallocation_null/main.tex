\title[]{The Elasticity of Substitution Across Carbon-Regulated Suppliers:\\ Evidence from the EU ETS in Belgian Firm-to-Firm Data}

\author[]{Gabriel Jardanovski\textsuperscript{*} \and Gert Bijnens\textsuperscript{$\dagger$}}
\thanks{\noindent \textsuperscript{*}Jardanovski (corresponding author): Northwestern University. E-mail: \texttt{gabrielj@u.northwestern.edu}.}
\thanks{\textsuperscript{$\dagger$} Bijnens: National Bank of Belgium. E-mail: \texttt{gert.bijnens@nbb.be}.}

\begin{abstract}
We estimate the elasticity of substitution between carbon-regulated and unregulated suppliers in Belgian firm-to-firm transactions over 2005--2022, using the post-2017 EU ETS price increment as the cost shock to regulated suppliers. The supplier-side relative-price signal is well-supported in our setting: a monthly panel local projection on the \citet{kanzig2023unequal} structural carbon-policy shock returns a peak PPI response of $+4.08$ (s.e.\ $0.64$) at 12 months, and a regulated-vs-unregulated event study with sector-specific linear trends returns Phase~2 and Phase~4 differential coefficients of $+0.094$ and $+0.113$. We take this as the price signal and ask how strongly Belgian buyers reallocate in response. We test two reallocation margins: (i) within a narrowly defined supplier sector (NACE 4-digit), across suppliers; and (ii) across NACE 4-digit input categories within the same buyer-year. Mapping our reduced-form coefficients into a CES nest, the within-NACE-4d margin gives a 95\% CI for $\sigma$ of $[0.74, 1.26]$ under our preferred (conservative) pass-through assumption $\rho = 0.5$ and $[0.87, 1.13]$ under full pass-through $\rho = 1$, with a point estimate at $\sigma \approx 1.0$ in both cases. The across-category margin gives a wider but consistent bound, $\sigma_{\text{cat}} \in [0.5, 5.0]$ centered at $1.05$. Our headline finding is that $\sigma$ is tightly identified in a neighborhood of Cobb-Douglas: relative quantities adjust one-for-one with relative prices (so substitution is operative), but expenditure shares are essentially invariant, and elasticities at the magnitudes typically estimated in import-substitution work are outside our 95\% confidence interval at both margins. We discuss five mechanisms consistent with $\sigma \approx 1$ --- shock magnitudes, relational capital, regulated-alternative saturation in heavy sectors, partial supplier-side pass-through, and buyer-side pass-through downstream --- and their implications for quantitative models of carbon policy in production networks.
\end{abstract}
