\title[]{Carbon Pricing and the Reallocation of Inputs:\\ A Null Result from Belgian Firm-to-Firm Data}

\author[]{Gabriel Jardanovski\textsuperscript{*} \and Gert Bijnens\textsuperscript{$\dagger$}}
\thanks{\noindent \textsuperscript{*}Jardanovski (corresponding author): Northwestern University. E-mail: \texttt{gabrielj@u.northwestern.edu}.}
\thanks{\textsuperscript{$\dagger$} Bijnens: National Bank of Belgium. E-mail: \texttt{gert.bijnens@nbb.be}.}

\begin{abstract}
A standard prediction of the carbon-leakage literature is that, when carbon pricing raises the marginal cost of regulated suppliers, downstream buyers reallocate purchases toward less-regulated alternatives. We test this prediction directly on Belgian firm-to-firm transaction data covering 2005--2022. We examine two reallocation margins: (i) within a narrowly defined supplier sector (NACE 4-digit), substitution from the most carbon-exposed ETS supplier toward the buyer's other suppliers in the same sector; and (ii) across NACE 4-digit input categories within the same buyer-year, substitution away from heavily regulated input categories toward less-regulated ones. Using two-way fixed-effects difference-in-differences and an event study with post-hoc detrending, we find no evidence of buyer reallocation at either margin. The most direct CMdG-style domestic analog (a binary regulated $\times$ \textit{Post} regression at the across-category margin) yields a coefficient of $-0.003$ (s.e.\ $0.009$); the most-favorable subset of the within-NACE-4d test (cells where the buyer holds the largest share of its input bill in the supplier's sector) yields a coefficient of $+0.03$ (s.e.\ $1.39$). A descriptive Grossman--Krueger decomposition delivers a complementary fact on the supplier side: within-sector reallocation accounts for essentially none of the 46\% decline in Belgian ETS emissions over 2005--2022. We interpret these as observational nulls subject to important caveats --- a parallel-trends violation in the within-NACE-4d test, a small but statistically detectable extensive-margin response, and limited power against modest substitution at the right tail of pair-shock magnitudes --- but the joint pattern across margins, sample subsets, and specifications is difficult to reconcile with first-order reallocation in response to Phase III/IV carbon prices.
\end{abstract}
