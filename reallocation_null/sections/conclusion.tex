We test whether Belgian downstream buyers reallocated their input mix in response to the EU ETS at two margins. On the within-NACE-4d, across-supplier margin, the headline pair-exposure regression is null (pooled $\beta = -16.7$, s.e.\ $17.4$; with linear-trend control $+5.4$, s.e.\ $20.4$, with the trend coefficient itself not statistically distinguishable from zero); the most-favorable subset (cells where the supplier's NACE 4-digit accounts for the largest share of the buyer's total input bill) yields $\beta = +0.03$ (s.e.\ $1.39$). On the across-NACE-4d, across-category margin, the binary regulated $\times$ \textit{Post} specification --- the most direct CMdG-style domestic analog --- yields $\beta = -0.003$ (s.e.\ $0.009$, $p = 0.76$); the year-by-year continuous-treatment event study shows uniformly positive coefficients in every clean post-2015 year, in the opposite direction from substitution.

Mapping the within-NACE-4d Q4 estimate into a CES elasticity gives $\sigma \in [0.87, 1.13]$ at the 95\% confidence level under full pass-through, with the point estimate at $\sigma = 1.0$; the larger sample tightens this to $[0.85, 1.03]$. The across-NACE-4d estimate gives $\sigma_{\text{cat}} \in [0.5, 5.0]$. The bounds are consistent with a Cobb-Douglas elasticity of substitution at both margins and rule out, at conventional confidence levels, the elasticities typically estimated in import-substitution work for differentiated intermediates. A standard Grossman--Krueger emissions decomposition on the same window shows that within-sector reallocation accounts for essentially zero of the 46\% aggregate emissions decline, consistent with the buyer-side null at the within-NACE-4d margin.

The data is consistent with several non-mutually-exclusive explanations for an elasticity centered near unity: shock magnitudes that are small at the typical pair and do not produce a detectable kink at the right tail; relational capital in Belgian B2B (pairs older than five years carry $\sim$79\% of total trade); the prevalence of regulated alternatives within the same NACE 4-digit in heavy-emitting sectors; partial supplier-side pass-through; and buyer-side pass-through onto downstream customers. We cannot separately identify these mechanisms with the current data. A small but statistically detectable extensive-margin response qualifies the intensive-margin null but does not overturn it.

We do not interpret these results as a structural statement about an underlying time-invariant elasticity of substitution between regulated and unregulated suppliers, or as a prediction that buyers \textit{would not} reallocate at higher carbon prices. Whether reallocation would appear at $\sim$2.5$\times$ the realized 2022 EUA price --- where right-tail pair-shock magnitudes would scale to match \citet{peter2023}'s p95 imported-input shock --- is a question we cannot answer with current data, and we leave it open.
